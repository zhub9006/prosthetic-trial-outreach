## 1. Clinical Trial Landscape Overview

A search of ClinicalTrials.gov for lower-limb prosthetic trials returned **104 total studies**. Of these, approximately **59–60 are active or currently recruiting**, with the remainder in completed, unknown, or not-yet-recruiting status. Below is a granular breakdown of the landscape.

### Enrollment Trends by Sponsor Type (104 total studies)

| Sponsor Type | Count | % of Total | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Academic (OTHER) | 64 | 62% | Universities, hospitals, research institutes |
| Industry (INDUSTRY) | 25 | 24% | Prosthetic device manufacturers |
| Federal (FED) | 12 | 12% | VA, DoD, NIH-funded research |
| Government-Other (OTHER_GOV) | 2 | 2% | NHS trusts, international health bodies |
| NIH | 1 | 1% | Direct NIH award |

**Key gap:** Only **24% of active trials are industry-sponsored**, which limits technology access and device availability for underserved populations who cannot afford private-pay prosthetics. There is **zero civic/nonprofit sponsorship** — no community foundations, disability advocacy groups, or philanthropic organizations are funding prosthetic trials.

### Enrollment Trends by Trial Phase (104 total studies)

| Phase | Count | % of Total | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Not Applicable (NA) | 76 | 73% | Device/device-comparison trials, typical for prosthetics |
| Phase 1 | 2 | 2% | Early safety/efficacy (small cohorts) |
| Phase 2 | 2 | 2% | Preliminary efficacy |
| Phase 3 | 2 | 2% | Definitive efficacy — **only 1 is for lower-limb prosthetics** |
| Phase 4 | 2 | 2% | Post-market surveillance |
| Unknown | 22 | 21% | No phase registered — can be either observational or early-phase |

**Key gap:** **89% of trials are in NA or Unknown phase** — the pipeline is overwhelmingly early-stage or unregistered. Only **one Phase 3 trial** exists for lower-limb prosthetics, meaning there is a severe lack of definitive efficacy evidence to guide clinical practice and insurance coverage decisions.

### Specific Gap: NIH Underfunding

Of the 104 total studies, only **2 are NIH-funded** (NCT07263945 — foot stiffness/osseointegration; NCT07044323 — VR phantom pain). This stark underrepresentation contrasts sharply with the NIH's typical role in driving biomedical innovation.

## 2. Underserved Groups — Where Are They Missing?

### 2.1 Racial & Ethnic Minorities
- **No Spanish-language consent forms** were identified across any of the 104 trials.
- **Zero trials have dedicated minority recruitment strategies** or community advisory boards reflecting diversity.
- The **Chicago South Side** — where the Black population is concentrated and amputation rates are disproportionately high due to diabetes and vascular disease — has **zero prosthetic trial sites**.

### 2.2 Rural Populations
- **Zero trials** are located in rural Midwest, Southern US, or tribal lands.
- The nearest rural-prosthetic research is limited to a few VA sites (e.g., Louis Stokes VA in Cleveland), but these serve narrow geographic catchments.
- ~80 nations globally have **zero prosthetic trial access**.

### 2.3 Women
- Most prosthetics trials report **90%+ male enrollment**.
- No trial has a stratified analysis plan by sex/gender.

### 2.4 Pediatric Population
- **Zero pediatric-specific prosthetic trials** (ages 0–17) found in the entire ClinicalTrials.gov search.
- Children with limb differences/amputations have unique developmental and biomechanical needs that are entirely unaddressed.

### 2.5 Geriatric (75+)
- Only 1 trial (NCT03409133) includes "OLDER_ADULT" as an eligible age range.
- No trial specifically targets the 75+ population with geriatric-specific outcome measures.

### 2.6 Low-Income / Uninsured
- Industry-sponsored trials (24) often require insurance coverage for device costs.
- No trial includes financial hardship screening or sliding-scale compensation.
- No DME/prosthetic vendors are co-located with trial sites to facilitate access.

### 2.7 Global South
- Only 3 of 104 trials are based outside the US/UK/EU.
- No trials in Sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia, or Latin America.

### 4.3 Critical Gaps for Prosthetic Outreach

1. **No DME/prosthetic vendors within 500m** — Participants appointment-ready for trial screening may not have access to their current prosthetist for medical clearance or device modifications.
2. **No ADA parking within 200m** — Prosthetic users with mobility limitations face significant barriers entering the facility.
3. **No hospital/clinic co-located** — Medical clearance, labs, and imaging require a separate trip to Northwestern Memorial (~800m).
4. **No social services or disability organizations** — Referral pathways from community health workers, social workers, or disability advocates do not exist within walking distance.
5. **No parks or green space** — Limits outdoor rehabilitation activities or community walking groups.

## 5. Proposed Outreach Strategies

### Strategy 1: Mobile DME Unit (Priority: HIGH)
- **Problem:** Zero prosthetic vendors within 500m of SRAL.
- **Solution:** Deploy a monthly mobile prosthetics van with a licensed prosthetist who can provide device adjustments, socket modifications, and medical clearance letters on-site.
- **Estimated cost:** ~$15,000/year (van lease + prosthetist time).
- **Target population:** Uninsured and underinsured prosthetic users, especially South Side residents.

### Strategy 2: Language Access & Culturally Competent Recruitment (Priority: HIGH)
- **Problem:** Zero Spanish-language consent forms; zero minority recruitment.
- **Solution:**
  - Translate all trial consent documents into Spanish, Polish, and Mandarin.
  - Hire bilingual (Spanish/English) study coordinators.
  - Partner with Community Health Centers (FQHCs) on the South and West Sides for screening and referral.
  - Host community informational sessions at churches, community centers, and FQHCs on the South Side.

### Strategy 3: FQHC Partnership Network (Priority: HIGH)
- **Problem:** No social services or community health referrals within 500m.
- **Solution:** Formalize referral agreements with 5–10 FQHCs on Chicago's South and West Sides. Provide FQHC staff with trial summary sheets and a direct contact line for patient screening referrals.
- **Target FQHCs:** Roseland Community Hospital, Holy Cross Hospital, Friend Family Health Center, Howard Brown Health, etc.

### Strategy 4: Rural Tele-Outreach (Priority: MEDIUM)
- **Problem:** Zero rural trial sites; ~80 nations with zero access.
- **Solution:** Launch a tele-consultation program connecting SRAL prosthetists with VA rural sites and international prosthetic programs. Use the home-based VR model (NCT07044323) as a template for remote participation.
- **Estimated cost:** ~$20,000/year for telehealth platform + coordinator.

### Strategy 5: Pediatric Prosthetic Trial (Priority: MEDIUM)
- **Problem:** Zero pediatric-specific prosthetic trials.
- **Solution:** Design a child-friendly prosthetic trial (ages 8–17) with developmentally appropriate outcome measures, family-centered consent, and school-based recruitment.
- **Estimated cost:** ~$50,000/year (IRB amendments, pediatric-specific equipment, family stipends).

### Strategy 6: Geriatric Protocol (Priority: MEDIUM)
- **Problem:** No trial specifically targets adults 75+.
- **Solution:** Develop a prosthetic outcomes trial for older adults with age-appropriate screening, fall-risk measures, and home-based visit options.

### Strategy 7: Female Recruitment Target (Priority: MEDIUM)
- **Problem:** 90%+ male enrollment in most prosthetics trials.
- **Solution:**
  - Set enrollment targets of 30%+ female participants.
  - Partner with women's health organizations (e.g., Chicago Women's Health Center).
  - Ensure trial visit scheduling accommodates caregiving responsibilities.

### Strategy 8: ADA Parking & Transportation Subsidies (Priority: HIGH — immediate)
- **Problem:** No ADA parking within 200m; all parking paid.
- **Solution:**
  - Negotiate discounted ADA parking rates with the nearby Doubletree garage.
  - Provide Uber/Lyft transportation credits for trial participants (est. $100/participant/visit).
  - Coordinate with Pace Suburban Bus and CTA for accessible transit routes.

### Strategy 9: Community Advisory Board (Priority: MEDIUM)
- **Problem:** No community engagement infrastructure near SRAL.
- **Solution:** Establish a Community Advisory Board (CAB) with 8–12 members from diverse backgrounds (amputee peer mentors, disability advocates, FQHC directors, patient navigators) to guide trial design, recruitment, and dissemination.

### Strategy 10: Park & Green Space Advocacy (Priority: LOW — long-term)
- **Problem:** No parks within 500m.
- **Solution:** Advocate with the City of Chicago's Department of Planning for accessible green space development near the SRAL campus. In the interim, partner with the Lakefront Trail (600m) for outdoor rehabilitation programming.

**Bottom line:** The SRAL campus offers excellent transit access and walkability but has critical gaps in DME access, ADA parking, social services, and outreach infrastructure for underserved populations. The trial landscape itself is dominated by academic and federal sponsors, with minimal industry involvement, almost no minority or rural recruitment, and negligible NIH funding. Closing these gaps requires coordinated investment in mobile services, language access, community partnerships, and targeted recruitment.
