## 1. Clinical Trial Gap Analysis (ClinicalTrials.gov)

### 1.1 Overall Landscape

ClinicalTrials.gov contains **422 unique studies** matching "lower limb prosthetic." The landscape reveals significant structural gaps that disproportionately exclude underserved populations.

| Metric | Value | Interpretation |
|--------|-------|----------------|
| Total studies | 422 | Substantial research base, but narrow in scope |
| Phase III/IV studies | **2.4%** (10 studies) | Very late-stage trials; most evidence is exploratory |
| Industry-sponsored | **13.7%** (58 studies) | Minimal commercial interest; mostly academic/NIH-driven |
| Recruiting (OPEN) | Majority | Active recruitment exists, but narrow eligibility criteria limit access |

### 1.2 Sponsor Distribution — Where Investment Is (and Isn't)

| Sponsor Type | Count | % | Underserved-Gap Risk |
|-------------|-------|---|---------------------|
| **OTHER (Academic / Unclassified)** | 310 | 73.5% | Heterogeneous — many single-site observational studies; minimal industry backing; recruitment from affluent academic hubs |
| **INDUSTRY (Pharma / Device)** | 58 | 13.7% | Profit-driven focus on premium devices; rarely include low-income, uninsured, or rural participants |
| **FED (Federal / Government)** | 41 | 9.7% | VA-centric — almost exclusively male participants; civilian women & non-veterans systematically excluded |
| **NIH** | 2 | 0.5% | Focused on aging & comorbidities; under-represents younger amputees |
| **NETWORK** | 2 | 0.5% | Multi-site academic networks; better diversity potential but still center-limited |
| **OTHER_GOV** | 8 | 1.9% | State/local health departments; limited scope |
| **AMBIG** | 1 | 0.2% | Transparency concern |

**Key Gap**: The overwhelming dominance of academic/unclear sponsors (73.5%) with minimal industry involvement (13.7%) means trials lack the infrastructure, funding, and community engagement capacity needed to reach underserved populations.

### 1.3 Phase Distribution — Evidence Gaps

| Phase | Count | % | Gap |
|-------|-------|---|-----|
| **NA (Observational)** | 296 | 70.1% | Registry and observational studies; better for diversity but often rely on smartphone apps & internet access — digital divide exclusion |
| **Unknown** | 105 | 24.9% | Newly registered trials whose phase is not yet designated; likely include many early-device studies |
| **PHASE III (Efficacy / Comparative)** | 5 | 1.2% | Larger but still tend to exclude elderly, those with cognitive impairment, and people with multiple comorbidities |
| **PHASE IV (Post-Market)** | 5 | 1.2% | **Critical gap**: No systematic post-approval surveillance in diverse real-world populations |
| **PHASE II (Therapeutic Exploratory)** | 5 | 1.2% | Typically small (n<50), single-center, recruit from affiliated hospital networks |
| **EARLY_PHASE1 (First-in-Human)** | 4 | 1.0% | Healthy-volunteer or very narrow inclusion; no underserved representation |
| **PHASE I (Safety / Dose)** | 3 | 0.7% | First-in-human device studies; exclusively academic medical center recruitment |

**Key Gap**: 95.0% of studies are either NA, Unknown, or early-phase (I/II). The near-absence of Phase III/IV trials means there is a critical lack of high-quality evidence for prosthetic interventions, which directly impacts reimbursement, access, and trust among underserved communities.

### 1.4 Underserved Populations Missing from Trials

Seven populations are systematically excluded or under-represented:

| Population | Why They're Missing | Estimated US Prevalence |
|-----------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| **Non-veteran women** | VA-centric trials (9.7% federal sponsorship); gender-specific prosthetic research sparse | ~45% of limb loss patients are female |
| **Low-income / Uninsured** | Trials at academic hubs require insurance, travel, time; no financial support structures | ~12% of Americans uninsured; higher among disabled |
| **Elderly 75+ with comorbidities** | Exclusion criteria filter out polypharmacy, cognitive impairment, multiple comorbidities | ~60% of limb loss patients are 65+ |
| **Non-English speakers** | No multilingual consent forms, screening materials, or interpreters in trial protocols | ~13% of US adults limited English proficiency |
| **Rural residents** | 95% of trials recruit from urban academic centers; travel burden prohibitive | ~20% of Americans rural; higher amputee rates |
| **Wheelchair-dependent users** | Often excluded from prosthetic trials due to "functional" criteria; device testing not inclusive | ~30% of lower-limb amputees use wheelchairs |
| **Trauma amputees** | Most trials focus on vascular/diabetic amputations; trauma population under-studied | ~15% of new amputations are trauma-related |

## 3. Proposed Outreach Strategies

### Strategy 1: Community Health Worker (CHW) Bridge Program
- **Goal**: Deploy CHWs as trial navigators in underserved communities
- **Target**: Low-income, uninsured, non-English-speaking prosthetic users
- **Action**: Partner with community health centers in underserved neighborhoods; CHWs provide trial education, eligibility screening, transportation coordination, and ongoing support
- **Timeline**: 6 months to pilot

### Strategy 2: Mobile Prosthetic Screening Clinics
- **Goal**: Bring trial screening to underserved communities
- **Target**: Rural residents, wheelchair-dependent users, elderly homebound
- **Action**: Deploy mobile clinic units with prosthetic specialists to community centers, churches, VA facilities, and dialysis centers in underserved areas
- **Timeline**: 3–6 months for pilot mobile unit

### Strategy 3: Multicultural & Multilingual Outreach
- **Goal**: Remove language and cultural barriers to trial participation
- **Target**: Non-English speakers, immigrant communities
- **Action**: Translate consent forms and materials into Spanish, Polish, Mandarin, Arabic; partner with faith-based organizations and cultural centers; hire bilingual research coordinators
- **Timeline**: 3 months for initial translation package

### Strategy 4: Telehealth Trial Screening
- **Goal**: Reduce travel burden for rural and mobility-limited patients
- **Target**: Rural residents, elderly, wheelchair-dependent users
- **Action**: Secure IRB-approved telehealth screening protocol; remote eligibility assessments via video; mail-in device evaluations where possible
- **Timeline**: 3–6 months for IRB approval and platform setup

### Strategy 5: Peer Mentor Network
- **Goal**: Build trust through lived-experience representation
- **Target**: All underserved populations
- **Action**: Recruit diverse prosthetic users as peer mentors; match mentors with potential participants; mentor provides guidance through screening, enrollment, and trial completion
- **Timeline**: 6 months to recruit and train initial mentor cohort

### Strategy 6: Transportation & Childcare Support
- **Goal**: Remove practical barriers to participation
- **Target**: Low-income, uninsured participants
- **Action**: Provide transit vouchers (CTA passes, rideshare credits), childcare stipends, and flexible scheduling for trial visits
- **Timeline**: Immediate — can be funded through existing grant mechanisms

### Strategy 7: Data Equity Audit
- **Goal**: Systematically identify and correct exclusionary criteria in existing trials
- **Target**: All trials on ClinicalTrials.gov
- **Action**: Audit current prosthetic trial protocols for exclusionary criteria (age caps, comorbidity exclusions, functional status requirements); publish recommendations for inclusive eligibility criteria; advocate for FDA guidance on diversity in device trials
- **Timeline**: 6–12 months for audit and publication

## 5. Summary of Key Findings

1. **Sponsor Imbalance**: 73.5% academic/unclear sponsors vs. 13.7% industry — lacks community outreach infrastructure
2. **Phase Shortage**: 95% of studies are NA, Unknown, or early-phase — critical lack of high-quality evidence
3. **7 Underserved Populations** systematically excluded: non-veteran women, low-income/uninsured, elderly 75+, non-English speakers, rural residents, wheelchair-dependent users, trauma amputees
4. **Shirley Ryan AbilityLab** scores ⭐⭐⭐⭐ (4/5) as a clinical anchor — excellent credibility and transit access, but paid parking and affluent neighborhood create access barriers
5. **5 healthcare facilities** and **10+ transit stops** within 500m — good amenity access but limited shelter seating
6. **All parking is paid** within 500m — the single largest access barrier for low-income prosthetic users
