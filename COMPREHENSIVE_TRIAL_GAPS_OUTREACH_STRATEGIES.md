## Part 3: Identified Gaps for Underserved Populations

### Gap #1: Racial & Ethnic Diversity
- Most trial protocols **do not report demographic breakdowns**
- NCT04427007 enrolled 100% White participants
- African American, Hispanic/Latino, Asian, and Indigenous populations are consistently underrepresented
- **Impact:** Prosthetic designs and rehabilitation protocols may not account for diverse body types, skin tones, or cultural preferences

### Gap #2: Geographic / Rural Access
- **Zero clinical trials based in rural Midwest or Southern US**
- Nearly all US-based studies target major urban academic centers (Chicago, Boston, Philadelphia, San Diego)
- Participants from rural areas face prohibitive travel costs, no local screening, and loss of income
- **Impact:** Rural amputees — who have higher rates of diabetes-related and vascular-related amputations — have no direct access to cutting-edge prosthetic trials

### Gap #3: Age Range Restrictions
- Many trials cap enrollment at ages 55–65; some as low as 18
- The average age of lower limb amputation in the US is 65+ (vascular disease, diabetes)
- **Impact:** Older adults — the largest segment of the prosthetic user population — are systematically excluded from research that should inform their care

### Gap #4: Sex / Gender Representation
- Sex-specific enrollment demographics are rarely required
- Several trial cohorts are male-skewed (e.g., trauma-related amputations recruiting from military populations)
- **Impact:** Female prosthetic users lack device and protocol data tailored to anatomical differences

### Gap #5: Insurance & Financial Barriers
- Industry-sponsored trials may require specific insurance coverage or out-of-pocket payment for unapproved devices
- Government/academic trials are more accessible but often lack device innovation
- **Impact:** Low-income uninsured/underinsured individuals cannot participate in the most innovative trials

### Gap #6: Lower-Limb Specificity
- Some trials (e.g., PEQ validation) aggregate upper + lower limb amputees
- Transtibial-focused trials exclude transfemoral users who have different mobility needs
- **Impact:** Device recommendations may not generalize across amputation levels

### Gap #7: Phase Pipeline Gap
- 98% of trials are "NA" or "Unknown" phase — meaning devices enter the market without a structured evidence pathway
- Only 1 Phase 1 and 1 Phase 4 study exist across 97 trials
- **Impact:** No rigorous transitional testing between prototype and market release; underserved populations often receive untested devices first

### Gap #8: Disability-Specific Accessibility
- Trial sites rarely assess or accommodate wheelchair/prosthetic mobility barriers in recruitment materials
- No trials explicitly report accessibility accommodations for participants with mobility impairments
- **Impact:** Trial participation itself can be a barrier for the very populations being studied

## Part 5: Proposed Outreach Strategies

### Strategy 1: Community-Based Mobile Screening Events
- **Target:** Rural populations (>50 miles), age 65+, racial/ethnic minorities, low-income uninsured
- **Approach:** Deploy mobile screening labs to community centers, churches, VA facilities, FQHCs in rural Midwest & Southern states
- **Shirley Ryan as Hub:** Use AbilityLab's infrastructure for satellite event staffing, IRB/regulatory support, and telemedicine connectivity
- **Timeline:** Pilot 3 events in first quarter (Chicago South Side, rural Illinois, rural Indiana)

### Strategy 2: Tele-Trial Participation Hybrid Model
- **In-Person:** Quarterly in-person visits at Shirley Ryan AbilityLab for device fitting, gait analysis, and outcome measurement
- **Telehealth:** Monthly virtual check-ins for adherence, adverse events, and prosthetics troubleshooting
- **Financial Support:** Travel stipends ($500/trip), lodging partnerships (NorthShore University HealthSystem network), mileage reimbursement IRS rate
- **Target:** >50-mile radius participants across Illinois, Indiana, Michigan, Wisconsin, Minnesota

### Strategy 3: Industry-Academic Partnership Pipeline
- Match prosthetic device manufacturers (Össur, Stryker, Ottobock, Flex-Foot/Blatchford) with FQHCs and university-affiliated community clinics
- Negotiate **pro bono device donations** for trial participants who cannot afford market-rate prosthetics
- Create an "Industry Device Bank" — returned or refurbished devices donated to underserved participants post-trial

### Strategy 4: Mandatory Diversity & Inclusion Benchmarks
- **Protocol Requirements:** All new trial protocols must include:
  - Minimum diversity targets: ≥25% female enrollment, ≥30% underrepresented minority enrollment, ≥15% age 65+ enrollment
  - Mandatory demographic reporting with public transparency dashboard
  - Accessibility accommodation reports in all trial-related materials
- **IRB Integration:** Work with IRB to mandate these diversity requirements as a condition of approval

### Strategy 5: Patient Navigation & Transportation Services
- Hire bilingual **Patient Navigators** to provide eligibility screening, enrollment support, and appointment coordination through study completion
- Partner with **Divvy** for adaptive cycling options & **CTA** for reduced-fare travel card enrollment (ADA paratransit, reduced monthly passes)
- Partner with **ride-share** for subsidized first/last-mile transportation to trial sites
- **Chicago-specific:** Establish a "Prosthetic Transit Hub" at Shirley Ryan with coordinated shuttle or accessible rideshare pickup

### Strategy 6: Older Adult & Caregiver Programming
- Advocate for protocol amendments to raise age caps to 85+ and remove arbitrary upper-age limits
- Provide **caregiver stipends** ($30/session) for participants requiring assistance with mobility, transportation, and communication
- On-site caregiver rest/support areas with WiFi, restrooms, and companion amenities

### Strategy 7: Rural Outreach via VA and Federal Health Centers
- Partner with **VA Hospitals** in Champaign, IL; Marion, IL; and Central Indiana — all have established amputee rehabilitation programs
- Partner with **FQHCs** in rural Southern Illinois, the Mississippi Delta, and Appalachia
- Deploy **mobile prosthetics labs** (trailer-mounted gait analysis and fitting stations) to rotate through these locations quarterly

### Strategy 8: Data Transparency & Community Accountability Dashboard
- Build open-source dashboard (published here on GitHub) displaying for every trial:
  - Enrollment demographics by race, ethnicity, sex, age
  - Geographic reach (distance from centroid)
  - Phase pipeline status
  - Accessibility accommodation levels
- Establish **Community Advisory Board** of 8–12 prosthetic users from underserved communities (minimum 4 racial/ethnic minority members, 4 rural residents, 4 age 65+)
- Biannual public report card with town halls at Shirley Ryan AbilityLab

## Appendix B: Q&A — Understanding the Gap Analysis

### Why is the Industry Sponsor gap (only 15%) significant?
Industry trials advance device technology and often provide free prosthetic devices, frequent monitoring, and premium care. When only 15% of trials are industry-sponsored, 85% of prosthetic users — disproportionately from underserved backgrounds — lack access to trial-grade prosthetic technologies and the intensive clinical support they provide.

### Why does the Phase 1–3 absence matter?
Without structured Phase 1 (safety) → Phase 2 (efficacy) → Phase 3 (comparative effectiveness) pathways, prosthetic devices enter the market with minimal rigorous testing. This disproportionately harms underserved users who may lack the information or resources to evaluate device quality and are more likely to receive devices through low-cost channels (e.g., VA, insurance default).

### Could rural participants feasibly enroll in Chicago-based trials?
Certainly not without intervention. The average rural Illinois participant faces 200–400 miles of one-way travel, ~$0.655/mile (IRS rate), ~$130–260 round trip — without lodging for multi-day visits. Mobile screening, tele-trial participation, and rural satellite sites (Strategies 1, 2, 7) are essential prerequisites.
