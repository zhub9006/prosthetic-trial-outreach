# Prosthetic Trial Outreach Assessment

## 1. Clinical Trial Gap Analysis

### Overview
A search of ClinicalTrials.gov for lower limb prosthetic trials reveals significant gaps in recruitment, sponsorship diversity, and phase distribution that point to underserved populations lacking representation in prosthetic innovation.

**Key Insight:** The overwhelming dominance of academic sponsors (73.4%) without corresponding industry or NIH backing means trials may lack funding for multi-site, diverse recruitment and community-based prosthetic users are unlikely to encounter trial information.

### Enrollment Trends by Phase (425 Total Studies)

| Phase | Count | % of Total | Gap Assessment |
|-------|-------|-----------|----------------|
| **N/A (Not Applicable / Device Feasibility)** | 298 | 69.2% | Most studies are early-stage device tests, not efficacy trials |
| **Unknown** | 106 | 24.9% | Nearly a quarter of all studies have unclear phase data |
| **Phase 2** | 5 | 1.2% | Only 5 efficacy/side-effect studies — critical gap |
| **Phase 3** | 5 | 1.2% | Only 5 large-scale confirmation trials — critical gap |
| **Phase 4** | 5 | 1.2% | Post-market surveillance is minimal |
| **Phase 1** | 3 | 0.7% | Early safety studies |
| **Early Phase 1** | 4 | 0.9% | First-in-human exploration |

**Key Insight:** 94.1% of studies are N/A, Unknown, or Early Phase 1. Only 2.4% are Phase 2+. Underrepresented groups (low-income, rural, minority, uninsured) are never reached because trials never advance to recruitment-heavy later stages.

### Identified Gaps for Underserved Populations

1. **Rural/Geographic Gap:** Academic-led trials concentrate at major research hospitals. Prosthetic users in underserved urban neighborhoods have no nearby trial sites.

2. **Economic Access Gap:** Industry-sponsored trials (13.6%) are most likely to provide the prosthesis at no cost. With so few industry trials, underserved users who cannot afford devices are excluded.

3. **Phase Gap:** Only 2.4% of studies are Phase 2+ — the phases where diverse recruitment strategies are typically implemented.

4. **Pediatric Gap:** Only 1 study targets children. Lower limb prosthetic needs for pediatric users are almost entirely unaddressed in clinical research.

5. **NIH/Public Research Gap:** With only 2 NIH-funded studies, federally supported research serving the public good is minimal.

6. **Diversity & Representation Gap:** No studies specifically mention outreach to minority, low-income, or uninsured prosthetic users. Eligibility criteria (e.g., K3+ functional level, age 18-70) systematically exclude the most impaired and oldest users.

## 3. Proposed Outreach Strategies

### Strategy 1: Community-Based Trial Navigation Hub
**Problem:** Academic-led trials (73.4% of sponsorship) are concentrated at research hospitals inaccessible to underserved users.
**Solution:** Establish the Shirley Ryan AbilityLab as a Prosthetic Trial Navigation Hub — a physical location where community members learn about active prosthetic trials through plain-language summaries, get screened for eligibility with lay-navigator assistance, receive transportation vouchers or ride-share credits for trial visits, and access prosthetist consultations that double as trial pre-screening.

### Strategy 2: Phase 2-4 Trial Development Pipeline
**Problem:** Only 2.4% of lower limb prosthetic studies are Phase 2+, meaning no proven, effective devices reach underserved communities.
**Solution:** Partner with NIH (only 0.5% of sponsorship) to fund at least 2 Phase 2 efficacy trials in the next 3 years specifically including low-income and minority prosthetic users. Require industry sponsors (13.6%) to include community advisory boards with prosthetic users from underserved neighborhoods. Advocate for NIH R01/R61 grants focused on closing the phase gap.

### Strategy 3: Pediatric Prosthetic Research Expansion
**Problem:** Only 1 of 425 studies (0.2%) targets pediatric prosthetic users.
**Solution:** Leverage Lurie Children's Hospital proximity to launch a pediatric prosthetic outcomes registry. Partner with schools and adaptive sports programs to identify children who need prosthetics research participation. Include family navigation support as a trial component.

### Strategy 4: Transit-Accessible Mobile Outreach
**Problem:** Even when trials exist, prosthetic users with limited mobility cannot travel to distant sites.
**Solution:** Use the 14+ CTA bus stops within 500m as outreach distribution points for trial information flyers in large-print and Braille. Partner with CTA for accessible transit vouchers specifically for trial participation. Deploy a mobile outreach van from the AbilityLab to South/West Side neighborhoods via paratransit routes.

### Strategy 5: Pharmacy & Primary Care Integration
**Problem:** Prosthetic users receive little trial information during routine healthcare visits.
**Solution:** Place trial information cards in pharmacies within 500m of the AbilityLab. Train pharmacy staff and primary care providers on clinical trial awareness for prosthetic users. Create a referral pathway: Doctor -> Pharmacy -> Trial Navigator -> AbilityLab.

### Strategy 6: Data Collection & Monitoring
**Solution:** Track demographic enrollment data across all prosthetic trials (race, income, insurance status, geography). Publish quarterly Prosthetic Trial Equity Dashboard on the GitHub repo. Set targets: 40% of trial participants from underserved communities within 5 years.
