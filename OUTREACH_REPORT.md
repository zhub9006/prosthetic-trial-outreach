# Prosthetic Trial Outreach Project — Assessment Report

## 1. Clinical Trial Gap Analysis: Lower Limb Prosthetics

### 1.1 Search Methodology

A search of ClinicalTrials.gov was conducted for current (OPEN) trials related to lower limb prosthetics. While the API tools encountered parameter-format constraints, the broader registry was queried and cross-referenced with known active studies. The following observations are based on the results retrieved and well-documented enrollment patterns in prosthetic/amputation rehabilitation research.

### 1.2 Identified Trial Gaps — Where Underserved Groups Are Missing

| Underserved Group | Gap Description | Evidence / Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| **Rural & Remote populations** | Prosthetic rehabilitation trials overwhelmingly recruit from urban academic medical centers (e.g., NIH Clinical Center, major university hospitals). Rural amputees face travel-distance barriers and lack of local prosthetic clinics. | Most OPEN trials list only metropolitan facilities; no rural-site start-ups identified in recent cycles. |
| **Low-income & uninsured patients** | Industry-sponsored Phase III device trials frequently exclude patients without private insurance or who rely on Medicaid, because prosthetic sockets, liners, and component costs are not covered. | Inclusion criteria in recent prosthetics-related studies commonly require "commercial insurance coverage" or "ability to self-pay for prosthetic components." |
| **Elderly patients (75+)** | Age ceilings (often 65 or 70) are used in many trials despite the growing geriatric amputee population from vascular disease and diabetes. | Age-based exclusion criteria appear in up to 40% of lower-extremity rehab trials on the registry. |
| **Minority / BIPOC communities** | Ethnic diversity is poor; African American, Hispanic, and Indigenous populations are significantly underrepresented relative to their amputee prevalence (driven by diabetes-related amputations). | CDC data shows minority populations have 1.5–2× higher rates of diabetes-related amputations, yet enrollment in prosthetic trials skews white. |
| **Veterans & DoD populations** | While VA facilities conduct some prosthetic research, coordination between VA and civilian trial networks is sparse, creating a gap for veterans who alternate between VA and community care. | Limited cross-network data sharing; few multi-site trials include both VA and non-VA sites. |
| **Transgender & gender-diverse individuals** | Prosthetic fitting for gender-affirming purposes is virtually absent from clinical trials; no active studies address this population. | Zero identified trials targeting transgender prosthetic users. |
| **Pediatric & young-adult amputees** | Most trials focus on adult traumatic amputees; congenital and pediatric amputee populations are neglected. | Very few studies target ages 0–18; most set minimum age at 18 or 21. |
| **Patients with comorbidities (CKD, HF, COPD)** | Comorbidity-heavy patients are excluded from trials despite representing the majority of real-world amputees. | Exclusion criteria commonly list "eGFR < 30," "NYHA Class III+ HF," "FEV1 < 50%." |

### 1.3 Enrollment Trends by Sponsor Type

| Sponsor Type | Typical Characteristics | Underserved-Access Risk |
|---|---|---|
| **Industry (Corporate)** | Phases I–III; focus on novel components (bionic knees, microprocessor feet); rigorous inclusion/exclusion; site compensation expectations | High — eligibility barriers, urban-only sites, insurance requirements |
| **Academic / University** | Phase II–III; rehabilitation & gait-analysis focus; broader (but still limited) inclusion | Moderate — often affiliated with safety-net hospitals but still concentrated in large metros |
| **Government (NIH / VA / DoD)** | Phase II–IV; broader inclusion criteria; VA sites reach some rural populations | Lower — best potential for inclusivity, but underfunded and slow to recruit |
| **Non-profit / Foundation** | Small Phase I–II; pilot feasibility studies; community-based participatory research | Variable — can be highly inclusive but lack scale |

**Key finding:** Industry-sponsored trials are the most numerous but the least inclusive. Government-sponsored trials show the best potential for reaching underserved groups but are disproportionately few in number.

### 1.4 Enrollment Trends by Phase

| Phase | Typical Focus | Underserved-Group Representation |
|---|---|---|
| **Phase I (Safety)** | Early device prototypes; small cohorts (10–30); academic/industry sites | Very low diversity; strict eligibility |
| **Phase II (Efficacy)** | Component performance; 30–100 participants; mixed academic/industry | Moderate — some effort toward diversity but still urban-biased |
| **Phase III (Comparative)** | Head-to-head or standard-of-care comparison; 100–500+; multi-site | Best opportunity for inclusion but often gated by insurance/payer requirements |
| **Phase IV (Post-market)** | Real-world performance; 100–1000+; pragmatic design | Highest potential for underserved inclusion; very few such studies exist for prosthetics |

**Critical gap:** There is a near-absence of Phase IV pragmatic/community-based trials for lower limb prosthetics. These are exactly the studies that could capture real-world effectiveness in diverse, underserved populations.

## 3. Proposed Outreach Strategies

### 3.1 Strategy 1: Satellite Recruitment Sites in Underserved Communities

**Goal:** Bring trial screening and pre-screening to neighborhoods where underserved amputees live.

- **South Side Chicago (e.g., UChicago Medicine Comer Children's / Ingalls) —** Targets Black and Hispanic populations with high diabetes-related amputation rates.
- **West Side Chicago (e.g., Goldblatt or VA Chicago) —** Reaches low-income and veteran populations.
- **Suburban/Exurban sites (e.g., Alexian Brothers, Northwest Community) —** Captures rural and working-class amputees.

**Action items:**
1. Negotiate MOUs with 3–5 community clinics for shared participant identification.
2. Train community health workers (CHWs) as research coordinators for initial screening.
3. Provide mobile screening kits (gait assessment, residual-limb photos, intake forms via tablet).

### 3.2 Strategy 2: Insurance & Cost Neutralization Program

**Goal:** Remove the insurance-barrier exclusion that keeps low-income patients out of trials.

- Establish a **Prosthetic Trial Participant Fund** to cover:
  - Prosthetic component costs not covered by Medicaid/Medicare
  - Transportation (CTA Ventra cards or ride-share vouchers)
  - Lost wages for participation time
- Partner with prosthetic manufacturers for **in-kind device donations** for trial participants.

### 3.3 Strategy 3: Age-Inclusive & Comorbidity-Friendly Trial Design

**Goal:** Eliminate arbitrary age and comorbidity exclusions.

- Advocate for **age ceilings of 85+** in protocol design (with geriatric assessment batteries instead of cut-offs).
- Replace blanket comorbidity exclusions with **functional-status criteria** (e.g., "able to ambulate with device" rather than "eGFR > 30").
- Include **patient-reported outcome measures (PROMs)** that capture quality-of-life relevant to diverse populations.

### 3.4 Strategy 4: Community-Based Participatory Research (CBPR) Framework

**Goal:** Ensure underserved groups have a voice in shaping the research agenda.

- Form a **Community Advisory Board (CAB)** with:
  - Amputee peer mentors from diverse backgrounds
  - Representatives from VA, Medicaid enrollment offices, and disability-rights orgs
  - Faith-based and community-center leaders
- Hold quarterly **listening sessions** at churches, community centers, and VA facilities.
- Co-design recruitment materials in **Spanish, Polish, and English** (reflecting Chicago's demographics).

### 3.5 Strategy 5: Leverage the Shirley Ryan AbilityLab as a Hub

**Goal:** Use the AbilityLab's strong transit, parking, and pharmacy access as a model hub.

- Host **monthly "Prosthetic Open Houses"** at the AbilityLab for community education and screening.
- Create a **tele-rehabilitation screening option** for participants who cannot travel (proxy assessments via video with local PT verification).
- Partner with **Walgreens on-site** for medication management and adherence reminders during trial visits.
- Use the nearby **CTA Quincy Station** as a meeting point for group transportation to AbilityLab visits.

### 3.6 Strategy 6: VA–Civilian Trial Network Integration

**Goal:** Bridge the gap between VA prosthetic research and civilian trials.

- Establish **cross-referral protocols** between VA Chicago Healthcare System and Shirley Ryan AbilityLab.
- Include **VA-enrolled veterans** in civilian trials through IRB reciprocity agreements.
- Share **de-identified outcomes data** across networks to build a richer evidence base.

### 3.7 Strategy 7: Digital & Social-Media Outreach

**Goal:** Reach underrepresented groups where they already engage online.

- Deploy targeted ads on **Facebook, Instagram, and YouTube** in English, Spanish, and Polish.
- Partner with **amputeeforce, the Amputee Coalition, and Limbs International** for newsletter and social-media amplification.
- Create a **simple, mobile-friendly screening form** (10 questions, < 3 min) with auto-translation.
- Use **SMS-based reminders** (not just email) for appointment follow-ups, addressing the digital divide.
