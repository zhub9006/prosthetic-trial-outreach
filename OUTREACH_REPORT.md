## 2. Enrollment Trend Analysis

### By Sponsor Type

| Sponsor Type | Count | Share |
|--------------|-------|-------|
| Other (academic/nonprofit) | 64 | 61.5% |
| Industry | 25 | 24.0% |
| Federal (FED) | 12 | 11.5% |
| Other Government | 2 | 1.9% |
| NIH | 1 | 0.9% |

**Key Gap:** The majority of trials are sponsored by academic institutions (61.5%) and industry (24.0%). Federal/NIH funding is minimal (12.4% combined), and there is **no community-based or patient-advocacy sponsorship**. This means underserved populations relying on public health systems or community clinics are unlikely to be reached through trial recruitment.

### By Phase

| Phase | Count | Share |
|-------|-------|-------|
| Not Applicable (devices/observational) | 76 | 73.1% |
| Phase 2 | 2 | 1.9% |
| Phase 3 | 2 | 1.9% |
| Phase 4 | 2 | 1.9% |
| Unknown | 22 | 21.2% |

**Key Gap:** The overwhelming majority (73.1%) are device/observational studies with no formal regulatory phase. Only **4 trials are in formal Phase 2–4 testing**. The 22 "Unknown" phase studies may include early exploratory work without formal phase designation, suggesting a gap in structured clinical progression from proof-of-concept to evidence generation.

- **Latin America is sparse** — only 1 study in Mexico.
- **Eastern Europe and Central Asia** have zero representation.

## 3. Underserved Groups Missing from Trials

| Underserved Population | Evidence of Gap |
|------------------------|-----------------|
| **Rural/low-income patients** | Most trials are at academic medical centers (Sunnybrook, VA, major European hospitals). No trials target community clinics, FQHCs, or rural rehabilitation facilities. |
| **Developing-world amputees** | Only 3 studies in Africa (Kenya) and 1 in the Middle East (Gaza). The vast majority of the world's amputee population in low-resource settings is unstudied. |
| **Elderly minority populations** | Trials like WiiNWalk and 3DPrintAbility focus on specific subpopulations but do not stratify by race/ethnicity. No dedicated trials for minority elderly amputees. |
| **Bilateral amputees** | Excluded from many trials (e.g., footwear study, WiiNWalk). Only a few include them. |
| **Non-English speakers** | Inclusion criteria in US trials routinely exclude those unable to communicate in English, systematically excluding immigrant and refugee populations. |
| **K1/K4+ amputees** | The powered knee study (NCT06194838) targets K2 only. K1 (household ambulators) and K4+ (high activity) populations lack dedicated device trials. |
| **Pediatric amputees** | Very few trials; pediatric-specific prosthetic research is sparse despite different developmental needs. |
| **Female amputees** | No gender-stratified analysis in any of the reviewed trials; disproportionate representation possible. |

## 5. Proposed Outreach Strategies

### Strategy 1: Community-Based Recruitment Partnerships
- **Partner with community health centers and FQHCs** on Chicago's North Side (e.g., Heartland Health Center, Erie Family Health Center) to reach rural and low-income amputees not currently served by academic-based trials.
- **Target the 61.5% "Other" sponsor trials** — these academic studies lack community outreach infrastructure and would benefit from our partnerships.

### Strategy 2: Multilingual & Culturally Responsive Outreach
- **Address the English-only exclusion** by providing multilingual consent forms and study materials (Spanish, Polish, Mandarin — the top non-English languages in Chicago).
- **Partner with immigrant/refugee service organizations** (e.g., Illinois Coalition for Immigrant and Refugee Rights, Centro de Trabajadores) to reach non-English-speaking amputees.

### Strategy 3: Developing-World Tele-Rehabilitation Trials
- **Leverage the 3DPrintAbility model** (NCT03517774 — which showed 3D-printed sockets work in developing countries) to launch remote/tele-rehabilitation trials targeting the Africa and South Asia gaps.
- **Use the AbilityLab's telehealth infrastructure** to connect prosthetic users in underserved geographies with US-based prosthetists.

### Strategy 4: Accessible Transit & Site Navigation Support
- **Advocate for tactile paving at CTA bus stops** near the AbilityLab — currently absent at several stops critical to prosthetic users.
- **Develop a paratransit resource guide** for prosthetic users traveling to the AbilityLab for trial participation, including CTA Travel Training and divisional paratransit information.
- **Coordinate with the 24/7 Walgreens pharmacy** (just outside 500m) and Whole Foods for refreshments during outreach events.

### Strategy 5: K1 & K4+ Device Trial Advocacy
- **Push for dedicated trials for K1 (household) and K4+ (high-activity) amputees**, which are missing from the current evidence base. The abilityLab's clinical expertise positions it as an ideal site.
- **Engage with the VA (12 FED-sponsored trials)** to encourage inclusion of underserved veteran populations in prosthetics research.

### Strategy 6: Parking & Accessibility Improvements
- **Reserve accessible parking spots** at the Doubletree multi-storey lot (160m from AbilityLab) for trial participants.
- **Advocate for accessible parking signage** and closer drop-off zones at the AbilityLab entrance.
- **Install temporary wayfinding signage** from the Michigan Avenue bus stops to the AbilityLab entrance for first-time visitors with mobility aids.

### Strategy 7: Elderly & Minority-Specific Outreach
- **Target the 50+ age demographic** (WiiNWalk model) with outreach through senior centers and AARP chapters on Chicago's North Side.
- **Partner with African American and Hispanic community organizations** to address the racial/ethnic data gap in prosthetics research.
