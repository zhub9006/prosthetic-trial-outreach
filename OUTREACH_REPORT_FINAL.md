## 3. Proposed Outreach Strategies

### Strategy 1: Mobile DME Prosthetic Clinic (Highest Priority)
**Problem:** Zero DME vendors within 500m of SRAL. Patients who travel to SRAL for trials often can't get local prosthetic fitting/support afterward.
- **Action:** Partner with a DME provider to deploy a **monthly mobile prosthetic van** to SRAL's parking structure or adjacent public lot.
- **Target population:** Uninsured/underinsured prosthetic users, veterans, rural referrals.
- **Estimated cost:** ~$15,000/year (van lease + prosthetist staffing) or in-kind partnership with祈福/arete DME vendors.
- **Quick win:** Approach Lombard Area Prosthetics and Orthotics or similar Chicago-area DME providers for a pilot 6-month mobile clinic.

### Strategy 2: Language & Health Literacy Access
**Problem:** Zero trials include non-English consent forms; Chicago's West Side and South Side communities are majority Spanish, Polish, and Arabic-speaking.
- **Action:**
  - Translate all trial consent/regulatory documents into Spanish, Polish, Arabic, and Simplified Chinese.
  - Hire bilingual community outreach coordinators (budget ~$60K/yr for 2 FTEs).
  - Partner with **National Spanish Health Council** and **Polish American Association** for referral pipelines.
- **Quick win:** Start with Spanish — the largest non-English language spoken by Chicago amputees — and add others in subsequent quarters.

### Strategy 3: FQHC & Safety-Net Hospital Partnerships
**Problem:** Trials only recruit from academic medical centers (Northwestern, Shirley Ryan, etc.). Low-income and Medicaid patients are systematically excluded.
- **Action:**
  - Establish formal referral agreements with **FQHCs (Federally Qualified Health Centers)** on Chicago's South and West Sides:
    -rocu Health Center (Bronzeville)
    - University of Chicago Medical Center neighborhood clinics
    - Sinai Urban Health Institute
  - Create a **"Prosthetic Trial Navigator"** role embedded at 2–3 FQHCs to screen, educate, and refer eligible patients.
- **Quick win:** Pilot with one FQHC; track referral-to-screening conversion rate over 6 months.

### Strategy 4: Rural Tele-Outreach & Virtual Screening
**Problem:** All current trials are in urban academic hubs; rural Illinois, Indiana, and Wisconsin amputees must travel hours.
- **Action:**
  - Launch a **tele-rehabilitation and virtual eligibility screening** platform.
  - Partner with **VA Illiana Health Care System** (Champaign, IL) and **Jesse Brown VA Medical Center** (Chicago) for veteran referrals.
  - Use the existing NCT06210620 PRO App infrastructure for **remote data collection**.
- **Quick win:** Set up a dedicated tele-trial landing page on clinic website with virtual consent workflow.

### Strategy 5: Pediatric & Family-Friendly Trial Design
**Problem:** No pediatric prosthetic trials; no trials involve children or adolescents.
- **Action:**
  - Design a **pediatric-adjacent trial** (ages 13+, with caregiver involvement) for transtibial amputees.
  - Partner with **Shirley Ryan AbilityLab's existing pediatric rehab programs** and **Ann & Robert H. Lurie Children's Hospital**.
  - Include **child life specialists** in the study team to reduce procedural anxiety.
- **Quick win:** Start with a small n=20 pilot study on 3D-printed pediatric sockets (leverage Sunnybrook NCT03517774 methodology).

### Strategy 6: Parking & ADA Access Improvement
**Problem:** No identified ADA parking within 200m; garage entries have low clearance.
- **Action:**
  - Negotiate **2–3 reserved ADA spots** in the Doubletree garage with clear signage.
  - Arrange **valet or shuttle service** from a closer lot for patients with mobility limitations during trial visits.
  - Install **tactile paving** at SRAL's main entrance and trial screening area.
- **Quick win:** Most impactful and lowest-cost intervention — reserved ADA spots can be arranged immediately with the garage operator.

### Strategy 7: Community Health Worker (CHW) Program
**Problem:** Prosthetic users from underserved communities often don't know trials exist; trust barriers with academic institutions.
- **Action:**
  - Train **5–8 Community Health Workers** from target neighborhoods (Pilsen, Humboldt Park, Englewood, South Shore) as "Trial Ambassadors."
  - Compensate CHWs with stipends ($500–$1,000/month) plus prosthetic supply gift cards.
  - CHWs conduct door-to-door, barbershop, church, and community center outreach.
- **Quick win:** Partner with **Chicago Community Trust** or **Robert Wood Johnson Foundation** CHW funding streams.

### Strategy 8: Mental Health & Cognitive Inclusion Protocol
**Problem:** Most trials exclude participants with cognitive impairment, PTSD, or severe mental health conditions — precisely the populations with highest amputation rates (veterans, trauma survivors).
- **Action:**
  - Develop an **inclusive consent protocol** using simplified language (6th-grade reading level) and visual aids.
  - Partner with **PTSD treatment programs** at Jesse Brown VA and **thresholds** (Chicago mental health org) for co-recruitment.
  - Establish an **ethical oversight module** for cognitively diverse participants.
- **Quick win:** Create one inclusive consent template and pilot it in the next trial amendment.

## 5. Summary

The current lower limb prosthetic trial landscape is heavily weighted toward **observational registries (73%)** and **academic single-site studies (62%)**, with **critical under-investment** in:
- Efficacy trials (only 4 Phase 2/3 studies out of 104)
- Underserved populations (rural, non-English, pediatric, low-income, cognitive diversity)
- NIH/federal funding (only 1 NIH-sponsored study)

**Shirley Ryan AbilityLab** is an excellent geographic hub for outreach (10/10 transit, excellent walkability, 24/7 pharmacy nearby), but **the absence of DME vendors and ADA parking within 500m** are the most actionable site-level barriers. The strategies above prioritize mobile DME, language access, FQHC pipelines, and community health workers as the fastest path to reaching underserved prosthetic users.
