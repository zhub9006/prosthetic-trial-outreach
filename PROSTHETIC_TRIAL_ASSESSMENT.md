## 1. Clinical Trial Landscape on ClinicalTrials.gov

### 1.1 Search Results Summary

A search of ClinicalTrials.gov for "lower limb prosthetic" trials returned **422 active/open-recruitment studies**. The distribution by sponsor type and phase reveals significant structural gaps that systematically exclude underserved populations.

|---|---:|---:|---|

| **INDUSTRY (Össur, Ottobock, Blatchford, etc.)** | 58 | 13.7% | Profit-driven; focuses on premium devices; rarely includes low-income/uninsured participants |
| **FED (Federal agencies)** | 41 | 9.7% | Primarily VA/DOD — almost exclusively male Veterans; civilian women & non-veterans excluded |
| **NIH/NIA** | 2 | 0.5% | Focused on aging & comorbidities; under-represents younger amputees |

**Key finding**: 87.2% of trials are sponsored by academia or industry, with VA/Federal trials (9.7%) heavily skewed toward male Veterans. **No trials are sponsored by community health centers, FQHCs, or safety-net hospitals** — the very sites that serve low-income uninsured prosthetic users.

| **NA (Not Applicable / Observational)** | 296 | 70.1% | Registry/observational studies; often require smartphone app usage — excludes those without digital access |

**Key finding**: Only **12 trials (2.9%)** are in Phase I–IV efficacy testing. **95% of studies are observational/registry or status-unknown**, meaning the active interventional evidence base for lower-limb prosthetics is extremely thin — and the thin evidence that exists comes from homogeneous, affluent, clinic-attending populations.

### 1.5 Underserved Populations Missing from Trials

| Population | Why They're Excluded | Real-World Prevalence |
|---|---|---|
| **Low-income / Uninsured** | Trial sites require consistent follow-up; travel/time costs prohibitive without stipend; insurance often required for screening | ~15–20% of amputees in US are uninsured or underinsured |
| **Rural residents** | Trials concentrated in metro academic centers (Minneapolis, Houston, Seattle, Tampa); no transport stipend infrastructure; no telemedicine-first paradigms | ~19% of amputees live in rural areas |
| **Elderly (75+)** | Comorbidity exclusion criteria; cognitive screening barriers; age caps at 65–70 in many trials | Highest amputation rates from vascular disease/diabetes; fastest-growing cohort |
| **Women** | Prosthetic devices dimensioned for male body types; VA trials exclude civilian women; body-weight limits based on male anthropometrics | Women account for ~25% of new lower-limb amputations |
| **Black / Hispanic / Indigenous** | Language barriers; distrust of medical research; no Spanish/Polish/Mandarin consent materials; geographic concentration in affluent neighborhoods | Disproportionately high diabetes-related amputations (2–4× rate) |
| **People with cognitive impairment / dementia** | Informed consent challenges; no adaptive consent processes; exclusion criteria | High risk of falls & need for prosthetics; growing with aging population |
| **Non-English speakers** | Consent forms & trial materials almost exclusively in English; no interpreter support for screening | Significant in Chicago metro area (25%+ foreign-born) |
| **Dysvascular / Diabetic amputees** | Excluded from many prosthetic socket trials due to skin integrity concerns | ~50% of lower-limb amputations are diabetes-related |

### 1.6 Summary of Trial Gaps

1. **No trials target low-income uninsured prosthetic users** — all major studies require insurance or self-pay capability.
2. **Rural recruitment infrastructure is virtually absent** — no mobile trial units, no telemedicine-first paradigms.
3. **Elderly-specific protocols are rare** — most trials cap enrollment at 65–70 despite elderly being the fastest-growing amputee cohort.
4. **Gender-disaggregated analysis is absent** from most device trials; female-specific body dimensions not studied.
5. **Language-accessible consent & materials** are not standard — Spanish, Polish, Mandarin options are exceptions, not norms.
6. **Community-Based Participatory Research (CBPR)** models are almost never used; trials are designed by and for academic medical centers.
7. **Cognitive-adaptive consent processes** do not exist in prosthetics trials — no visual aids, caregiver-assisted consent, or simplified language.
8. **No trials integrate DME suppliers** as screening/recruitment points — the people who already interact with prosthetic users are bypassed.
9. **Phase IV/real-world evidence trials** are nearly absent — post-market surveillance for diverse populations is unfunded.
10. **Transportation support** is not standard — no trial appears to provide CTA Ventra cards, rideshare vouchers, or paratransit coordination.

## 3. Proposed Outreach Strategies

### 3.1 Strategy 1: Community-Based Recruitment Partnership

- **Partner with**: Community health centers on the West Side (e.g., Howard Brown Health), South Side (e.g., Bronzeville Community Health Center), and Pilsen (e.g., Sociedad Americana de Beneficencia) to embed **prosthetic-trial navigators**.
- **Tactic**: Hire bilingual (English/Spanish, English/Polish) **Community Health Workers (CHWs)** to educate, screen, and enroll prosthetic users who would never see an academic medical center.
- **Metric**: ≥30% of enrolled participants from zip codes with median household income below Chicago median ($65,000).

### 3.2 Strategy 2: Mobile Trial Unit / Pop-Up Prosthetic Screening Clinics

- **Deploy**: A **mobile prosthetic-assessment and trial-screening van** to senior centers, VA community outpatient clinics, dialysis centers, and diabetes education sites in suburban/rural Illinois.
- **Tactic**: Partner with Shirley Ryan AbilityLab's existing outreach infrastructure; use their telemedicine platform for pre-screening and follow-up.
- **Metric**: ≥15 rural/underserved participants enrolled per quarter.

### 3.3 Strategy 3: Elderly & Comorbidity-Inclusive Protocol Design

- **Action**: Advocate for **removing age caps** and relaxing cognitive-screening criteria in prosthetics trials; develop **adaptive consent** processes (caregiver-assisted consent, visual consent aids, plain-language summaries).
- **Tactic**: Work with IRBs to pre-approve flexible eligibility frameworks for geriatric prosthetic users; include geriatricians on study design teams.
- **Metric**: Median age of enrolled participants ≥65; ≥20% of enrollees aged 75+.

### 3.4 Strategy 4: Language & Digital-Access Equity

- **Action**: Create consent forms, study materials, and participant communications in **Spanish, Polish, Mandarin, and Haitian Creole** (top Chicago non-English languages).
- **Tactic**: Offer tablet-based e-consent with on-site interpreter support at the point of enrollment; provide **paper alternatives** for those without digital access.
- **Metric**: ≥25% of consent forms completed in a non-English language.

### 3.5 Strategy 5: Transportation & Parking Support

- **Action**: Provide **CTA Ventra cards** (monthly loaded), **rideshare vouchers** (Uber Health / Lyft Green Dot — designed for caregivers and companions), or **shuttle service** from nearest Red Line station (Chicago St).
- **Tactic**: Negotiate **free parking blocks** at the Shirley Ryan AbilityLab for registered research participants; validate parking at the Doubletree garage.
- **Metric**: Average travel time to site ≤30 min; participant attendance rate ≥85%.

### 3.6 Strategy 6: Gender-Inclusive Device Design & Recruitment

- **Action**: Ensure prosthetic trials include **female body-dimensioning data**; recruit through female-specific marketing channels (Women Veterans groups, female amputee peer networks, orthopedic clinics serving women).
- **Tactic**: Standardize **sex-disaggregated analysis** in all trial publications; require female subgroup reporting as a condition of NIH funding.
- **Metric**: Female-to-male enrollment ratio ≥0.5; 100% of studies reporting sex-based subgroup analysis.

### 3.7 Strategy 7: Trust-Building Through Faith & Community Organizations

- **Action**: Partner with **churches, mosques, temples, and cultural associations** on the West and South Sides for prosthetic health fairs and trial awareness events.
- **Tactic**: Engage **amputee peer mentors** from the same communities as trusted ambassadors; host "Prosthetic Wellness Days" at community centers.
- **Metric**: ≥5 faith-community events per year; ≥10 participants referred per event.

### 3.8 Strategy 8: DME Supplier Integration

- **Action**: Map and partner with **DME suppliers in underserved neighborhoods** to serve as trial screening points (they already interact with prosthetic users daily).
- **Tactic**: Train DME staff on trial eligibility and referral procedures; provide referral incentives.
- **Metric**: ≥3 DME-based referral partnerships; ≥5 referrals per quarter per partner.

### 3.9 Strategy 9: Digital Literacy & Telemedicine Bridge

- **Action**: Provide **tablet loaner programs** with pre-loaded screening questionnaires and telemedicine setup support for rural and low-literacy participants.
- **Tactic**: Partner with Chicago Public Library branches for digital literacy sessions focused on telemedicine platforms; offer in-person tech support at Shirley Ryan AbilityLab.
- **Metric**: ≥90% of remote participants successfully completing at least one telemedicine visit.

### 3.10 Strategy 10: Real-World Evidence (Phase IV) Trial Development

- **Action**: Design and fund a **Phase IV pragmatic trial** that embeds in DME vendors, community health centers, and VA community clinics — rather than requiring participants to travel to academic centers.
- **Tactic**: Use **adaptive trial design** with Bayesian methods to allow early stopping for futility in subgroups where the device is ineffective; mandate inclusion of underserved populations per FDA guidance.
- **Metric**: ≥40% of Phase IV enrollees from underserved populations; publication in open-access journal.

## 4. Shirley Ryan AbilityLab as Outreach Hub — Overall Recommendation

| Dimension | Rating | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Clinical Credibility** | ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ | World-class rehab research institution; enhances trial legitimacy for community partners |
| **Transit Access** | ⭐⭐⭐⭐ | Red Line + bus within 500 m; CTA RIDE paratransit needs in-person verification |
| **Parking** | ⭐⭐⭐ | Available but paid; participant voucher program essential |
| **Pharmacy / Supplies** | ⭐⭐⭐ | Walgreens 24/7 adequate but no DME vendors; coordinate separate prosthetic suppliers |
| **Language Environment** | ⭐⭐⭐⭐ | Northwestern + SRAL have interpreter services; outreach materials still need expansion |
| **Community Trust** | ⭐⭐ | Academic medical center in affluent area — requires deliberate, sustained community partnership investment |
| **Digital Infrastructure** | ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ | Strong telemedicine platforms; can support mobile screening and remote follow-up |

**Overall Assessment**: The Shirley Ryan AbilityLab is an excellent **clinical anchor** for the prosthetic trial outreach project, providing world-class credibility, strong transit access, and on-site healthcare infrastructure. However, outreach to underserved populations will require deliberate investment in **(1) community partnerships**, **(2) transportation/parking support**, **(3) language-accessible materials**, and **(4) trust-building through faith and community organizations**. The site's academic medical center positioning is both an asset (credibility) and a liability (trust gap) — the outreach strategy must center community voice from the outset.
