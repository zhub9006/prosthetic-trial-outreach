## 2. Trial Gaps by Sponsor Type

Analysis of 514 lower-limb prosthetic studies on ClinicalTrials.gov reveals a heavily skewed sponsor landscape:

| Sponsor Type | Count | % of Total | Implications for Underserved Groups |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Other (Academic/University)** | 367 | **71.4%** | High volume but limited capacity for underserved recruitment; focused on basic science |
| **Industry** | 90 | **17.5%** | Device companies focus on commercial markets; rarely target rural, uninsured, or minority populations |
| **Federal (FED)** | 45 | **8.8%** | VA/DoD trials better serve veterans but rarely extend to civilians in underserved areas |
| **Other Gov** | 8 | **1.6%** | State/local studies — very few |
| **NIH** | 1 | **0.2%** | ⚠️ Critical gap: Only 1 NIH-funded study for this entire category |
| **Network** | 2 | **0.4%** | Minimal multi-site collaboration |
| **Ambiguous** | 1 | **0.2%** | Unknown sponsor intent |

### Key Findings:
- **Zero civic/nonprofit-funded trials** — community organizations are entirely absent from sponsor roles
- **NIH underrepresentation** — only 0.2% federal funding; NIH typically mandates diversity requirements that would benefit underserved populations
- **Industry = commercial focus** — 17.5% of studies but these target commercially viable prosthetics (insurance-reimbursed), not cost-prosthetic solutions for uninsured
- **Academic dominance** — 71% means most trials require academic infrastructure that underserved communities lack access to

## 6. Proposed Outreach Strategies

### Strategy 1: Mobile DME & Prosthetic Supply Unit
**Priority: CRITICAL**
**Problem:** 0 DME vendors within 500m; uninsured amputees cannot access supplies
**Actions:**
- Launch a monthly mobile prosthetist van for uninsured individuals (~$15K/yr)
- Provide free emergency adjustments, socket checks, and fitting assistance
- Stock basic prosthetic supplies (sheaths, liners, socks) from pooled industry donations
- Partner with SRAL clinicians for on-the-spot consultations
- Target: Uninsured and Medicaid-eligible amputees in Streeterville, Gold Coast, Old Town

### Strategy 2: Language Access & Cultural Competency
**Priority: HIGH**
**Problem:** 0% of prosthetic trials offer non-English consent forms; Chicago is a major multilingual city
**Actions:**
- Translate all trial materials into Spanish, Polish, Arabic, Chinese (Mandarin/Cantonese)
- Hire bilingual community health workers as trial navigators
- Offer evening/weekend clinic hours for hourly workers
- Demographic equity dashboard tracking enrollment vs. Chicago demographics
- Cultural competency training for SRAL outreach staff (focus on immigrant amputee communities)
- Cost: ~$25K for translation + staffing

### Strategy 3: FQHC & Community Health Center Partnerships
**Priority: CRITICAL**
**Problem:** No FQHCs or community clinics within 500m of SRAL
**Actions:**
- Establish referral pipeline with FQHCs on South/West Side (e.g., Roseland, Englewood, Humboldt Park)
- SRAL staff travel to FQHCs for monthly outreach clinics
- Create "screening + referral" system where FQHCs identify eligible prosthetic trial candidates
- Partner with VA Illinois Health Care System for veteran amputee outreach
- Cost: ~$40K/yr for staffing and coordination

### Strategy 4: Rural & Tele-Rehabilitation Outreach
**Priority: HIGH**
**Problem:** 0 rural trial sites; 71% academic-sponsored trials require urban academic infrastructure
**Actions:**
- Virtual screening visits via tele-rehabilitation platforms
- Monthly in-person telehealth kiosks at rural libraries, VA clinics, and FQHCs
- Ship trial devices to participants with home-based PT coaching
- Partner with Illinois Farm Bureau and rural health networks
- Cost: ~$30K/yr for tele-rehabilitation infrastructure

### Strategy 5: Pediatric & Age-Inclusive Trial Design
**Priority: HIGH**
**Problem:** 0 pediatric prosthetic trials; no age-stratified design
**Actions:**
- Design a new pediatric prosthetic trial with elder-inclusive age ranges (10–85)
- Create child-friendly prosthetic trial protocols with fun, engaging consent/assent processes
- Include parental support and transportation assistance for pediatric participants
- Partner with Shriners Hospitals for Children (Chicago) and Ann & Robert H. Lurie Children's Hospital
- Cost: ~$250K startup grant (NIH R01-level proposal)

### Strategy 6: SRAL On-Site Resource Corner
**Priority: MEDIUM**
**Problem:** SRAL lacks a dedicated community outreach space for prosthetic users
**Actions:**
- Dedicate a physical corner/room at SRAL as a prosthetic resource hub
- Stock DME samples, insurance navigation guides, and walk-in adjustment clinic hours
- Host monthly "Amputee Community Hour" with peer mentors and trial recruiters
- Partner with local disability advocacy organizations (e.g., Amputee Coalition of Illinois)
- Cost: ~$10K startup for setup + ongoing modest staffing

### Strategy 7: Address Parking & Parking-Related Barriers
**Priority: MEDIUM**
**Problem:** No ADA-compliant parking within 200m; low-clearance garages
**Actions:**
- Negotiate reserved ADA parking spots at Doubletree garage (162m away)
- Provide valet shuttle service from ADA parking to SRAL for prosthetic users
- Add clear wayfinding signage from SRAL to nearest ADA parking
- Partner with Chicago Parking Meters LLC for ADA drop-off zone at Erie St entrance

### Strategy 8: 24/7 Divvy Adaptive Cycling Program
**Priority: LOW**
**Problem:** Divvy bike stations exist but no adaptive cycling program
**Actions:**
- Partner with Divvy to provide adaptive cycles (handcycles, trikes) at nearby stations
- Create a "Cycle-to-Trial" program where prosthetic users can bike to SRAL for appointments
- Include adaptive cycling in prosthetic rehabilitation trials as an outcome measure
