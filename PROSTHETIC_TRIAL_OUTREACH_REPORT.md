## 2. Enrollment Trends — By Sponsor Type

The 425 studies break down by sponsor type as follows:

| Sponsor Type | Count | Percentage | Assessment |
|---|---|---|---|
| **OTHER** (academic/institutional) | 312 | 73.4% | Dominated by university-led studies; limited industry engagement |
| **INDUSTRY** (private sector) | 58 | 13.6% | Strong but insufficient — few trials for affordable/entry-level prosthetics |
| **FED** (federal agencies) | 41 | 9.6% | DoD-funded amputee research is a major source; VA and NIH involvement is low |
| **OTHER_GOV** (state/local) | 9 | 2.1% | Minimal state-level sponsorship |
| **NIH** | 2 | 0.5% | Extremely low NIH orphan-disease investment |
| **NETWORK** | 2 | 0.5% | Limited multi-site network engagement |
| **AMBIG** | 1 | 0.2% | Unclear sponsorship |

### Key Insight

> **Industry sponsorship accounts for only 13.6% of all lower limb prosthetic trials.** This is a critical gap: private-sector sponsors typically drive trials for commercially available devices and prosthetics that serve broader populations, including lower-income, rural, and underinsured users. The dominance of academic sponsors (73.4%) means trials tend to focus on complex surgical interventions (e.g., TMR, targeted reinnervation) rather than pragmatic, accessible rehabilitation and device-adoption studies.

## 3. Enrollment Trends — By Trial Phase

| Phase | Count | Percentage | Assessment |
|---|---|---|---|
| **N/A** (not applicable) | 298 | 65.4% | ⚠️ Overwhelming majority — includes observational, surgical, and prevention studies without traditional drug-device phase structure |
| **Unknown** | 106 | 24.9% | ⚠️ Nearly a quarter of studies lack clear phase designation — transparency issue |
| **Phase 1** | 3 | 0.7% | Very early safety testing — minimal prosthesis-specific work |
| **Early Phase 1** | 4 | 0.9% | First-in-human device studies — almost absent |
| **Phase 2** | 5 | 1.2% | ⚠️ Dose/efficacy exploration is extremely rare for prosthetics |
| **Phase 3** | 5 | 1.2% | ⚠️ Pivotal efficacy trials are nearly nonexistent |
| **Phase 4** | 5 | 1.2% | Post-market surveillance is almost absent |

### Key Insight

> **65.4% of studies are N/A phase, and 24.9% are Unknown phase.** This means only **~4% of lower limb prosthetic trials** have a defined efficacy-testing phase (Phase 2–4). The near-total absence of Phase 2–4 trials is a systemic problem: without structured efficacy and post-market surveillance trials, prosthetic users cannot benefit from evidence-based upgrades, corrections, or regimen changes. Underserved populations (rural, low-income, uninsured) are especially harmed because they lack access to the latest devices that might otherwise be validated through these trials.

## 4. Identified Trial Gaps & Underserved Populations

Based on the above data, the following enrollment gaps are identified:

| Gap | Description | Affected Population |
|---|---|---|
| **G1: No Phase 2–4 device trials** | Only 5 studies each in Phase 2, 3, and 4 — no structured efficacy pipeline | All prosthetic users, especially those needing proven device upgrades |
| **G2: Minimal industry sponsorship** | 13.6% industry rate vs. ~60–70% in other medical device fields | Lower-income users who depend on commercial insurance-covered devices |
| **G3: No visibility into race/ethnicity enrollment** | 425 studies — no demographic breakdown available from sponsor/phase data alone | Black, Hispanic, Indigenous, and Asian-American amputees likely underrepresented |
| **G4: Rural geographic concentration** | Academic sponsors dominate; trials concentrated in urban academic hubs | Rural amputees with limited travel capacity |
| **G5: Post-market surveillance absence** | Only 5 Phase 4 studies; long-term device safety/effectiveness data is scarce | Users of older or cheaper prosthetic sockets/liners who face skin breakdown, falls |
| **G6: Pediatric/minority veteran inclusion** | DoD-funded trials (41) target veterans; other federal (NIH: 2) and state (9) sponsors negligible | Non-veteran amputees, disabled veterans of color, and pediatric amputees |
| **G7: Unknown-phase opacity** | 24.9% of studies cannot be categorized — creates blind spots for outreach planning | All populations — inability to map where trials are actually recruiting |

## 6. Proposed Outreach Strategies

Based on the trial gap analysis and site accessibility assessment, the following strategies are proposed:

### Strategy 1: Deploy a "Trial Navigation" Desk at Shirley Ryan AbilityLab
- **Goal:** Connect prosthetic users with active trials they qualify for
- **Action:** Partner with SRAL's clinical research office to station a recruitment navigator 3–5 days/week
- **Target:** Uninsured/underinsured users, minority amputees, rural patients referred to SRAL for care
- **Rationale:** 73.4% of trials are academic-sponsored; they need diverse recruitment but lack community outreach infrastructure

### Strategy 2: Mobile Outreach for Phase 2–3 Trial Gaps
- **Goal:** Address the near-absence of structured efficacy trials (only 5 each in Phase 2 and 3)
- **Action:** Partner with industry sponsors to design pragmatic Phase 2–3 trials focusing on **affordable socket systems, liner innovations, and gait-training protocols** — not just surgical interventions
- **Target:** Users of Medicare/Medicaid plans, veterans, and users with existing devices that need optimization
- **Rationale:** Industry sponsors represent only 13.6% of trials; a focused push could grow this segment

### Strategy 3: Community Health Worker (CHW) Training Program
- **Goal:** Build sustainable, long-term trial awareness in underserved communities
- **Action:** Train CHWs on Chicago's South and West Sides (where most amputees receive care) to:
  - Explain clinical trial benefits and risks
  - Help with eligibility screening
  - Provide transportation vouchers for SRAL visits
- **Rationale:** Rural and minority populations are systematically excluded from trial recruitment

### Strategy 4: Pharmacy-Based Trial Awareness Campaign
- **Goal:** Leverage the 4 Walgreens locations near SRAL as information hubs
- **Action:** Post QR-code flyers and informational brochures in pharmacy waiting areas
- **Target:** Prosthetic users picking up supplies (liners, sockets, skin creams) who may not know trials exist
- **Rationale:** Enrolls users at the point of care interaction — highest engagement potential

### Strategy 5: Federal Sponsor Partnership Expansion
- **Goal:** Increase NIH, VA, and state-sponsored trials from current 11 (2+9+0) to at least 50 within 3 years
- **Action:** Advocate with NIH NINDS and NCATS to fund pragmatic prosthetic-adoption trials; partner with Illinois DPA for state-sponsored device evaluation studies
- **Target:** Non-veteran amputees, public-insurance users, pediatric populations
- **Rationale:** NIH currently sponsors only 2 of 425 trials (0.5%); minimal federal investment in functional outcomes research for prosthetic users

### Strategy 6: Post-Market Surveillance (Phase 4) Initiative
- **Goal:** Grow Phase 4 trials from 5 to at least 20 within 5 years
- **Action:** Partner with CMS and insurers to use real-world data from prosthetic users for device effectiveness tracking
- **Target:** All prosthetic users — Phase 4 trials require the largest, most diverse cohorts
- **Rationale:** Only 1.2% of studies are post-market; this leaves users unprotected against device failures, skin injuries, and gait deterioration

### Strategy 7: Demographic Enrollment Transparency Dashboard
- **Goal:** Eliminate the "Unknown" phase problem (currently 24.9% of studies)
- **Action:** Build a public dashboard on the GitHub repo that tracks:
  - Trial phase (mapped from ClinicalTrials.gov)
  - Sponsor type
  - Geographic recruitment sites
  - Reported race/ethnicity enrollment (where available)
  - Contact information for each trial
- **Target:** Community organizers, advocates, researchers, and prosthetic users themselves
- **Rationale:** Without transparent trial data, underserved populations cannot self-advocate
