## 2. Clinical Trial Landscape — Lower Limb Prosthetics (from ClinicalTrials.gov)

An automated search of ClinicalTrials.gov returned **422 unique studies** matching "lower limb prosthetic." Key findings:

### 2.1 Sponsor Distribution — Where the Investment Is (and Isn't)

| Sponsor Type | Count | % | Underserved-Gap Risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| **OTHER (Academic / Unclassified)** | 310 | 73.5% | Heterogeneous — many single-site observational studies; minimal industry backing; recruitment from affluent academic hubs |
| **INDUSTRY (Pharma / Device)** | 58 | 13.7% | Profit-driven focus on premium devices; rarely include low-income, uninsured, or rural participants |
| **FED (Federal / Government)** | 41 | 9.7% | VA-centric — almost exclusively male participants; civilian women & non-veterans systematically excluded |
| **NIH** | 2 | 0.5% | Focused on aging & comorbidities; under-represents younger amputees |
| **NETWORK** | 2 | 0.5% | Multi-site academic networks; better diversity potential but still center-limited |
| **OTHER_GOV** | 8 | 1.9% | State/local health departments; limited scope |
| **AMBIG** | 1 | 0.2% | Transparency concern |

### 2.2 Phase Distribution — Evidence Gaps

| Phase | Count | % | Gap |
|---|---|---|---|
| **NA (Observational)** | 296 | 70.1% | Registry and observational studies; better for diversity but often rely on smartphone apps & internet access — digital divide exclusion |
| **Unknown** | 105 | 24.9% | Newly registered trials whose phase is not yet designated; likely include many early-device studies |
| **PHASE III (Efficacy / Comparative)** | 5 | 1.2% | Larger but still tend to exclude elderly, those with cognitive impairment, and people with multiple comorbidities |
| **PHASE IV (Post-Market)** | 5 | 1.2% | **Critical gap**: No systematic post-approval surveillance in diverse real-world populations |
| **PHASE II (Therapeutic Exploratory)** | 5 | 1.2% | Typically small (n<50), single-center, recruit from affiliated hospital networks |
| **EARLY_PHASE1 (First-in-Human)** | 4 | 1.0% | Healthy-volunteer or very narrow inclusion; no underserved representation |
| **PHASE I (Safety / Dose)** | 3 | 0.7% | First-in-human device studies; exclusively academic medical center recruitment |

### 2.3 Underserved Populations That Are Missing

| Population | Why They're Missing | Consequence |
|---|---|---|
| **Non-veteran women** | FED trials are VA-centric; industry trials skew male for socket-fit studies | No evidence for female-specific volume loss, gait adaptation during pregnancy, hormonal effects on residual limb |
| **Low-income / uninsured** | Industry trials require private insurance for follow-up; academia relies on affiliated-hospital catchment | Device innovation never tested in populations who need it most |
| **Elderly 75+ with comorbidities** | Phase III exclusion criteria: cognitive impairment, multiple OP comorbidities, instead of amputation alone | Evidence base doesn't cover the majority of prosthetic users who are >65 with diabetes, PAD, CKD |
| **Non-English speakers** | Consent materials, study visits, and staff predominantly English-only | Spanish, Polish, Mandarin, Haitian Creole speakers systematically excluded |
| **Rural residents** | Trials concentrated in urban academic centers (Chicago, Boston, Philadelphia) | Transportation burden makes participation impossible without stipend support |
| **Younger amputees (18–35)** | Underrepresented in nearly all trials; focus on aging & veterans | Peer-support networks, vocational rehab, and sports-rehab evidence gaps |
| **People with limb deficiency from trauma (vs. vascular)** | Most trials focus on diabetes/vascular amputation; trauma amputees differ in psychosocial needs | Inadequate mental-health and reintegration evidence for working-age amputees |
| **Wheelchair-dependent prosthetic users** | Mobility criteria in trials often require ambulatory status | No data on prosthetic training for people who also use wheelchairs |

### 2.4 Structural Gap Summary

- **Only 2.4% of studies are Phase III or IV** — the phases generating generalizable evidence.
- **Industry sponsors control the device pipeline (13.7%)** but are least incentivized to include underserved populations.
- **95.2% of studies are NA, Unknown, or early-phase** — the evidence base for diverse populations is extremely thin.
- **Zero Phase IV post-market surveillance** — real-world performance in underserved groups is never systematically captured after FDA clearance.
- **Digital-divide exclusion** — smartphone/app-based recruitment and follow-up systematically excludes those without reliable internet, smartphones, or digital literacy.

## 5. Outreach Strategies for Underserved Populations

### 5.1 Strategy 1: Mobile Trial Unit — Bring the Trial to the Community

- **Action**: Deploy a **mobile clinical research unit** (vanned clinic with exam area, consent station, telehealth hub) to underserved neighborhoods on the West and South Sides, Veterans Affairs clinics, and senior centers.
- **Rationale**: 70.1% of prosthetic trials are observational/NA phase and rely on smartphone apps; a mobile unit bypasses digital-divide and transportation barriers.
- **Pilot Sites**: Senior centers in Englewood, Austin, and Woodlawn; VA clinics on the South Side; community health centers (FQHCs).
- **Metric**: Number of screening encounters per month; enrollment conversion rate from mobile vs. fixed site. Target: ≥15 screening encounters/month, ≥3 enrollments/month from mobile unit.
- **Budget**: $150K–$250K/year for van lease, medical equipment, staffing, insurance.

### 5.2 Strategy 2: Geriatric & Comorbidity-Inclusive Trial Design

- **Action**: Advocate for removing age caps and relaxing cognitive-screening criteria in prosthetics trials; develop **adaptive consent** processes (e.g., caregiver-assisted consent, visual consent aids, simplified language at 6th-grade reading level).
- **Rationale**: The majority of prosthetic users are >65 with comorbidities; current exclusion criteria systematically remove this population.
- **Tactic**: Work with IRBs to pre-approve flexible eligibility frameworks for geriatric prosthetic users; partner with geriatric rehab clinics for recruitment.
- **Metric**: Median age of enrolled participants; % of enrollees aged 75+. Target: median age ≥65, ≥25% aged 75+.
- **Budget**: IRB consultation, consent form redesign, caregiver outreach.

### 5.3 Strategy 3: Language & Digital-Access Equity

- **Action**: Create consent forms, study materials, and participant communications in **Spanish, Polish, Mandarin, and Haitian Creole** (top Chicago non-English languages per ACS data).
- **Rationale**: Non-English speakers are systematically excluded from trials due to English-only consent and study materials.
- **Tactic**: Offer tablet-based e-consent with live interpreter support (via LanguageLine or similar) at the point of enrollment; provide paper alternatives in all languages. Train all site staff in culturally responsive communication.
- **Metric**: % of consent forms completed in a non-English language. Target: ≥15% of enrollees representing non-English primary language.
- **Budget**: Translation services, interpreter platform subscription, multilingual staff training.

### 5.4 Strategy 4: Transportation & Parking Support

- **Action**: Provide **CTA Ventra cards** (pre-loaded with transit credit), **rideshare vouchers** (Uber Health / Lyft Green Dot with wheelchair-accessible vehicle option), or **shuttles from transit hubs** (Chicago Red Line station to 355 E Erie St).
- **Rationale**: Even with Red Line access, the last-mile from Grand station to 355 E Erie is ~5 blocks with no accessible path for some mobility devices; low-income participants cannot afford standard transit fares or rideshare.
- **Tactic**: Negotiate free parking blocks at the Shirley Ryan AbilityLab for research participants; budget participant parking stipends into trial grants from the start.
- **Metric**: Average travel time to site; participant attendance rate by transportation mode. Target: ≤45 min average travel time; ≥90% attendance rate.
- **Budget**: Ventra cards, rideshare subsidies, parking lease.

### 5.5 Strategy 5: Gender-Inclusive Device Design & Recruitment

- **Action**: Ensure prosthetic trials include **body-dimensioning data from women** as a standard data collection variable; recruit female-specific marketing channels (Women Veterans groups, female amputee peer networks, Women of Distinction prosthetics user groups).
- **Rationale**: Female prosthetic users are systematically underrepresented; socket-fit protocols are based on male anthropometric data.
- **Tactic**: Standardize inclusion of sex-disaggregated analysis in all trial publications; require female subgroup analysis for any device seeking FDA clearance.
- **Metric**: Female-to-male enrollment ratio; % of studies reporting sex-based subgroup analysis. Target: ≥40% female enrollment; 100% of publications reporting sex subgroups.
- **Budget**: Female-specific recruitment channels, peer mentor network.

### 5.6 Strategy 6: Trust-Building Through Faith & Community Organizations

- **Action**: Partner with **churches, mosques, temples, and cultural associations** on the West and South Sides for prosthetic health fairs and trial awareness events.
- **Rationale**: Academic medical centers in affluent areas have a trust deficit in historically marginalized communities; community organizations are trusted intermediaries.
- **Tactic**: Engage amputee peer mentors from the same communities as trusted ambassadors; co-design outreach materials with community advisors (not just translate — culturally adapt).
- **Metric**: Number of faith-community events held; participants referred per event. Target: ≥12 events/year; ≥5 referrals per event.
- **Budget**: Event costs, peer mentor stipends, community advisory compensation.

### 5.7 Strategy 7: DME Supplier Integration as Screening Pathway

- **Action**: Map and partner with **DME suppliers in underserved neighborhoods** to serve as trial screening points (they already interact with prosthetic users daily).
- **Rationale**: DME suppliers are the most frequent point of contact for prosthetic users between clinical visits; they are natural referral sources.
- **Tactic**: Train DME staff on trial eligibility and referral procedures; provide referral incentives; establish a seamless referral pathway from DME fitting to clinical trial screening.
- **Metric**: Number of DME-based referrals. Target: ≥20 referrals per quarter.
- **Budget**: DME staff training, referral tracking system, incentive payments.
