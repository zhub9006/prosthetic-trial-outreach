### 1.2 Enrollment Trends by Sponsor Type

*(Based on 33 active/recruiting/invited studies from ClinicalTrials.gov)*

| Sponsor Type | Count | Share | Key Concern |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Academic (OTHER)** | 19 | 58% | Strong research base but limited capacity for underserved recruitment |
| **Industry** | 8 | 24% | Technology access but profit-driven; rarely targets low-income or un/underinsured |
| **Federal (FED)** | 5 | 15% | VA and NIH-funded; most likely to reach veterans and diverse populations — but under-resourced |
| **Other Government (OTHER_GOV)** | 1 | 3% | Minimal civic/nonprofit presence |

**Key Gap:** Only ~22% of trials are industry-sponsored (providing device access) and **no civic or nonprofit sponsors exist** in the current active set. Federal sponsorship is concentrated in VA studies, which inherently exclude non-veteran populations. There is **no community-health-center or FQHC sponsor** — the entity most capable of reaching underserved groups.

### 1.3 Enrollment Trends by Phase

| Phase | Count | Share |
|---|---|---|
| **Phase I / II / III** | **0** | 0% |
| **Not Applicable (NA)** | 27 | 82% |
| **Unknown** | 6 | 18% |

**Critical Gap:** Nearly all lower-limb prosthetic trials on ClinicalTrials.gov are observational cohorts or feasibility studies. **There is only ~1 Phase 3 interventional trial** across the entire ~450-trial historical record. This means:
- Very few trials test **new prosthetic technologies** at scale
- No efficacy-stage trials platform recruitment of underserved populations
- The trial pipeline is heavily weighted toward **basic science and biomechanics**, missing the community-impact phase where outreach matters most

| Gap | Evidence | Impact |
|---|---|---|

## 3. Proposed Outreach Strategies

### 3.1 Priority 1: Mobile DME & Prosthetic Supply Unit

**Problem:** Zero prosthetic/DME vendors within 500 m of SRAL. Uninsured and underinsured amputees cannot access devices.

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| Approach | Partner with 2–3 DME suppliers to run a **monthly mobile prosthetic clinic** at SRAL's own facility (utilizing their community event space) |
| Services | Device fitting, maintenance, socket adjustments, liner replacements, basic orthotic assessments |
| Target | Uninsured, Medicaid, and underinsured prosthetic users in North Side, South Side, and West Side Chicago |
| Estimated cost | ~$15,000/year (vehicle lease + staff time + supplies) |
| Revenue model | Grant-funded; in-kind DME donations; CPT billing for covered services |

### 3.2 Priority 2: Language Access & South Side Recruitment

**Problem:** No Spanish-language consent forms; zero trial sites on Chicago's South Side (predominantly Black neighborhoods).

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| Approach | Translate all SRAL trial materials into Spanish; hire bilingual community health workers (promotoras) |
| Partnerships | Community health centers on South Side: Klinic Community Health, Bronzeville Community Health Center, Roseland Community Hospital |
| Strategy | Monthly "prosthetic health fairs" at South Side CBOs, rotating venues |
| Estimated cost | ~$25,000/year (translation, staffing, event logistics) |

### 3.3 Priority 3: FQHC & Community Health Center Partnerships

**Problem:** No FQHC sponsor in any active trial; community health centers are the natural point of contact for underserved amputees.

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| Approach | Establish SRAL as a **referral and assessment site** for FQHCs; lay out a "warm handoff" pipeline |
| Target FQHCs | CommunityHealth (Loop), Patient Centered Health Care, Erie Family Health Centers |
| Action | Train FQHC staff on trial eligibility; create a standardized referral form |
| Estimated cost | ~$10,000/year (training materials, coordination staff time) |

### 3.4 Priority 4: Rural Tele-Outreach

**Problem:** Zero prosthetic trials in rural areas. Rural amputees face travel barriers to Chicago-based trials.

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| Approach | Virtual consult and screening program connecting SRAL prosthetists with VA rural sites and FQHCs in downstate IL, southern IL, and western IL |
| Technology | HIPAA-compliant video platform; remote gait assessment via smartphone |
| Target | Rural veterans, agricultural workers, small-town residents with limb loss |
| Estimated cost | ~$20,000/year (platform licensing, equipment kits) |

### 3.5 Priority 5: Pediatric Prosthetic Trial Design

**Problem:** No pediatric-specific prosthetic trials exist nationally.

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| Approach | Design and propose a **child-friendly prosthetic outcomes trial** (ages 8–17) through SRAL's Pediatric Giggle Lab infrastructure |
| Differentiators | Child-friendly protocols, play-based outcome measures, family-centered recruitment, school-based outreach |
| Estimated cost | ~$50,000 (seed funding for protocol development; larger grant for full trial) |

### 3.6 Priority 6: Geriatric Prosthetic Protocol

**Problem:** No trials dedicated to adults 75+, the fastest-growing amputee segment.

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| Approach | Develop a **geriatric-focused prosthetic outcomes protocol** at SRAL, leveraging its post-acute rehab expertise |
| Focus | Fit-to-function in elderly, comorbidities, fall risk, cognitive load during adaptation |
| Estimated cost | ~$30,000 (protocol development + pilot data collection) |

### 3.7 Priority 7: Female Recruitment Initiative

| Element | Detail |

## 4. Summary & Recommendations

| Dimension | Assessment | Action |
|---|---|---|
| **Trial pipeline** | Flat — almost no Phase III studies; 100% observational | Advocate for NIH/NIBIB to fund efficacy-stage prosthetics trials |
| **Sponsor diversity** | No civic/nonprofit sponsor; heavy reliance on academia & VA | Recruit FQHCs and community organizations as co-sponsors |
| **Geographic reach** | Concentrated in urban academic centers | Deploy tele-outreach; satellite screening at rural VA and community sites |
| **Demographic reach** | Male, adult, veteran-skewed | Target women, children, elderly, and non-veterans through dedicated outreach |
| **SRAL as outreach site** | Strong transit, good ADA; weak on DME, parking, parks, social services | Address infrastructure gaps; leverage existing transit advantage |

**Bottom line:** The Shirley Ryan AbilityLab site has excellent transit access and ADA compliance, making it a viable community outreach hub — but it lacks DME vendors, social services, and parks within walking distance. The broader trial landscape is overwhelmingly academic and observational, with zero phase-III efficacy trials and no sponsors representing community or civic organizations. Outreach strategies that combine **mobile DME, language access, FQHC partnerships, and tele-outreach** can bridge these gaps.
