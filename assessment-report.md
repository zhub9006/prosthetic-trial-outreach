Federal sponsorship (NIH/VA) accounts for only ~14%, suggesting that federally funded prosthetic research — often with broader inclusion mandates — is underrepresented relative to the population need.

- **No mature, large-scale efficacy trials** exist for most lower limb prosthetic interventions.
- The pipeline is heavily skewed toward feasibility and early device testing, meaning **underserved populations rarely reach even Phase 2 enrollment**.
- Phase 3/4 trials — where generalizability and diverse enrollment matter most — are almost nonexistent for prosthetic devices.

## 2. Enrollment Gap Analysis — Where Underserved Groups Are Missing

### 2.1 Geographic Exclusion

| Gap | Evidence |
|-----|----------|
| **U.S. rural & Southern states** | Trial sites concentrate in Seattle (VA), San Diego (UCSD/LIMBER), Baltimore (Johns Hopkins). No active prosthetic-device trials in the Deep South, Appalachia, or rural Midwest — regions with high diabetes-related amputation rates. |
| **Non-U.S. low-resource countries** | 4 of 10 sampled trials are in France, Italy, or Russia. No trials in Sub-Saharan Africa, South/Southeast Asia, or Latin America — regions with the highest global prosthetic need (35–40M people). |
| **Canada limited to BC/ON** | SMART trial restricts enrollment to British Columbia and Ontario, excluding rural provinces and francophone communities. |

### 2.2 Demographic Exclusion

| Gap | Evidence |
|-----|----------|
| **Pediatric amputees** | 9 of 10 trials restrict enrollment to ages ≥18. Only EROMEGA includes ages ≥14. Zero trials target children — a population that needs frequent prosthetic replacements due to growth. |
| **Elderly >75** | Most trials cap at 65–75. LIMBER excludes >75 "due to fall risk." Yet adults >75 represent a growing amputation demographic (vascular/diabetes). |
| **Diabetic patients** | LIMBER explicitly excludes diabetics. Yet diabetes is the #1 cause of lower limb amputation in the U.S. (~54% of cases). |
| **Assistive-device users** | NCT06711588 excludes anyone using a cane/walker. This eliminates patients with higher-level amputations or comorbidities — precisely those most underserved. |
| **Non-English speakers** | SMART requires English fluency. ADJUST requires French fluency. No multilingual outreach is described in any trial. |
| **Bilateral amputees** | Most trials require unilateral amputation. Bilateral amputees — who face the greatest mobility barriers — are systematically excluded. |
| **People with mental health conditions** | Neuromodulation trial excludes anyone with psychiatric history or depression. Mental health conditions are prevalent among amputees. |

### 2.3 Socioeconomic Barriers

| Barrier | Impact |
|---------|--------|
| **Travel burden** | LIMBER requires living within 50 miles of UCSD. Most trials are single-site. Travel costs disproportionately affect low-income and rural patients. |
| **No transportation support** | Zero trials in our sample offer travel reimbursement, lodging assistance, or remote participation options (except SMART's tablet-based approach). |
| **Digital access** | SMART requires tablet + internet access, excluding digitally disconnected populations. |
| **Insurance/status requirements** | No trials describe assistance for uninsured or underinsured participants, despite prosthetic costs often exceeding $50,000. |

## 4. Proposed Outreach Strategies

### 4.1 Addressing Enrollment Gaps

| Strategy | Target Gap | Implementation |
|----------|-----------|----------------|
| **Multi-site expansion** | Geographic exclusion | Partner with VA hospitals in the South (e.g., Birmingham AL, Jackson MS) and rural Midwest (e.g., Omaha NE) to create satellite enrollment sites leveraging existing VA prosthetic clinics. |
| **Pediatric pipeline** | Children excluded | Advocate for a dedicated pediatric prosthetic trial track at Shirley Ryan AbilityLab (which already has pediatric rehab services). Target ages 6–17 with growth-adaptive prosthetic designs. |
| **Diabetic-inclusive protocols** | #1 amputation cause excluded | Work with LIMBER Prosthetics and similar companies to amend exclusion criteria. Design safety monitoring protocols that allow controlled enrollment of stable diabetic patients. |
| **Bilateral amputee studies** | Highest-need group excluded | Propose a new interventional study specifically for bilateral transtibial/transfemoral amputees, leveraging the AbilityLab's bilateral prosthetic fitting expertise. |
| **Elderly ≥75 inclusion** | Growing demographic excluded | Create a "frailty-adapted" trial arm with modified outcome measures (e.g., 2-minute walk test instead of 6-minute; supervised gait assessment). Partner with geriatric medicine at Northwestern. |
| **Remote/hybrid participation** | Travel burden & digital access | Develop a telehealth-enabled trial protocol: initial fitting at AbilityLab, follow-up assessments via remote video + wearable sensors (accelerometers). Provide loaner tablets + data plans for participants without internet. |
| **Multilingual outreach** | Non-English speakers excluded | Translate consent forms and study materials into Spanish, Mandarin, Arabic, and Hindi. Hire bilingual research coordinators. Partner with community health centers serving immigrant populations in Chicago. |
| **Travel & lodging fund** | Socioeconomic barriers | Establish a participant travel fund covering: CTA transit passes, hotel nights for multi-day visits, mileage reimbursement. Budget $500–$1,000 per participant. |

## 5. Summary

The lower limb prosthetic trial landscape is dominated by small, single-site, early-phase device studies led by academic sponsors. **Underserved populations — including diabetic patients, bilateral amputees, elderly >75, children, non-English speakers, and rural residents — are systematically excluded from nearly all active trials.** The Shirley Ryan AbilityLab in Chicago offers an exceptionally well-connected outreach site with 4 pharmacies, 10+ CTA bus stops, 6 Divvy bike-share stations, adequate parking, and co-location with Northwestern Memorial Hospital. By combining site leverage with targeted strategies addressing each identified gap, this project can meaningfully expand prosthetic trial enrollment equity.
