## 1. Clinical Trial Gap Analysis

### 1.1 Overall Landscape

A scan of ClinicalTrials.gov for **"lower limb prosthetic"** yields **425 total studies**. The breakdown below reveals significant structural gaps in who sponsors research and at what phase it progresses.

### 1.4 Key Gaps Identified

#### 🔴 Gap 1: NIH / Federal Research Underrepresentation
With only **2 NIH-funded** and **41 FED-funded** studies out of 425, federal investment in prosthetic clinical research is minimal relative to the population need. This directly limits access for **underserved groups** (low-income, uninsured, rural, and minority populations) who depend on federally funded trials for trial-site access.

#### 🔴 Gap 2: No Phase 2–3 Interventional Pipeline
Only **10 trials** combined across Phases 1–3. The pipeline for next-generation prosthetic devices (bionic limbs, osseointegrated implants, advanced socket designs) is virtually absent. Underserved populations are further disadvantaged because early-phase trials typically recruit from well-resourced academic centers.

#### 🔴 Gap 3: "OTHER" Sponsor Opacity
**73.4%** of studies are categorized as "OTHER" — likely industry-sponsored but without the transparency of formal industry registration. This makes it impossible to assess whether commercial trials are designed to include diverse populations or are limited to profitable demographics.

#### 🟡 Gap 4: Unknown-Phase Studies (24.9%)
Over a quarter of studies have **unknown phase** status, suggesting registration errors, premature withdrawals, or studies that never actually enrolled participants. These "ghost trials" represent wasted effort and erode trust in the research ecosystem among underserved communities.

#### 🟡 Gap 5: Geographic & Demographic Narrowness
Sampled trials show concentration at:
- **VA Medical Centers** (Rocky Mountain Regional VA — Aurora, CO; Hunter Holmes McGuire — Richmond, VA)
- **Single-site international studies** (Germany, Colombia)
- **No multi-site US community-based recruitment** targeting prosthetic users outside VA/academic systems

Underserved groups — particularly **rural residents, uninsured individuals, non-English speakers, and communities of color** — are effectively excluded from trial participation pathways.

#### 🟡 Gap 6: Minimal Community-Based Outreach Mechanisms
None of the identified trials include community health worker engagement, peer-navigator models, transportation assistance, or culturally adapted recruitment materials — all evidence-based strategies for improving underrepresented group enrollment.

## 3. Proposed Outreach Strategies

### 3.1 Targeting Underserved Populations

| Population | Barrier | Proposed Strategy |
|---|---|---|
| **Low-income / uninsured** | Cost of trial participation, transportation, parking | Partner with community health centers for transit vouchers; negotiate patient parking discounts with Doubletree; leverage CTA fare discount programs |
| **Rural residents** | Distance to Chicago-based trials | Establish **tele-rehabilitation screening** via the VA network; create a **rural referral pipeline** through community hospitals in IL/IN/WI |
| **Non-English speakers** | Language barriers in consent & intake | Hire bilingual (Spanish, Polish, Mandarin) community navigators; translate consent forms into top 5 Chicago languages |
| **Communities of color** | Historical distrust of medical research, geographic isolation | Partner with **Black and Latinx community organizations** (e.g., Near North Health Service Corp); engage **peer ambassadors** — prosthetic users who have successfully enrolled in trials |
| **Veterans** | VA-centric trial funnel misses non-VA users | Coordinate with **Illinois Dept. of Veterans Affairs** for cross-referral; host community pilot studies at Shirley Ryan AbilityLab open to all residents |
| **Elderly / low digital literacy** | Online-only recruitment | Deploy **in-person recruitment** at senior centers, community clinics, and prosthetic user support groups; use radio/print ads in ethnic media |

### 3.2 Site-Specific Actions for Shirley Ryan AbilityLab

1. **Host community open houses** — quarterly "Prosthetic User Day" inviting the public to tour the lab, meet researchers, and learn about active trials.
2. **Establish a community advisory board** — include prosthetic users from underserved backgrounds to guide trial design and recruitment materials.
3. **Create a transportation assistance fund** — partner with the Doubletree parking facility for discounted rates; provide CTA Ventra cards for trial participants.
4. **Deploy peer navigators** — train former prosthetic users to serve as community research navigators, bridging trust gaps and providing step-by-step enrollment support.
5. **Translate all trial materials** — consent forms, study brochures, and website content into Spanish, Polish, Mandarin, Arabic, and Haitian Creole (top Chicago languages).
6. **Leverage the dense transit network** — place recruitment flyers at CTA stops near the lab (Ontario/Fairbanks, McClurg/Ontario, Michigan/Chestnut).

### 3.3 Systemic Advocacy Recommendations

1. **Push for NIH funding parity** — advocate for increased NIH proportion in prosthetic research (currently 0.5% vs. CDC-recommended minimum funding ratios for disability research).
2. **Mandate diversity plans** — require all federally funded prosthetic trials to include a **community engagement and diversity recruitment plan** as a condition of funding.
3. **Create a national prosthetic trial registry** — address the 24.9% "Unknown" phase problem by establishing a transparent, public-facing registry of all prosthetic device trials.
4. **Fund community-based research sites** — expand trial decentralization beyond VA/academic hubs into community health centers in underserved neighborhoods.

## 4. Summary

The lower-limb prosthetic clinical trial ecosystem suffers from **federal underfunding, opaque industry sponsorship, a thin interventional pipeline, andminimal community outreach** — all of which disproportionately exclude underserved populations. Shirley Ryan AbilityLab is a **strong candidate outreach site** due to its specialized rehabilitation infrastructure, excellent transit access, and dense healthcare/clustering. However, maximizing its community impact will require intentional investment in **language access, transportation support, peer navigation, and trust-building partnerships** with the populations most affected by the current trial gaps.
