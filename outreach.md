# Prosthetic Trial Outreach Project

## 1. Clinical Trial Landscape Assessment

### Overall Search Results
A search of ClinicalTrials.gov for **"lower limb prosthetic"** returned **425 total studies**. Below is an analysis of enrollment patterns by sponsor type and trial phase, revealing significant gaps where underserved populations are missing.

---

### Enrollment Trends by Sponsor Type

| Sponsor Type | Count | % of Total | Key Insight |
|---|---|---|---|
| **OTHER** (non-profit/academic) | 312 | 73.4% | Dominated by university and research-hospital protocols |
| **INDUSTRY** (commercial) | 58 | 13.6% | Device manufacturers — limited community reach |
| **FED** (government/federal) | 41 | 9.6% | VA and other federal studies — focus on veteran populations |
| **OTHER_GOV** | 9 | 2.1% | Narrow scope |
| **NETWORK** | 2 | 0.5% | Very few multi-site networks |
| **NIH** | 2 | 0.5% | Minimal NIH-sponsored prosthetic research |
| **AMBIG** | 1 | 0.2% | Unclear sponsor |

#### 🔴 Underservice Gaps by Sponsor
- **Non-veteran adults are underserved.** The heavy FED/VA skew (41 studies) means trials disproportionately target **veterans and active-duty service members**. Civilians with lower limb amputations — especially those without VA access — have far fewer trial options.
- **Industry-sponsored trials are scarce** (only 58). This signals limited commercial engagement with community-based prosthetic users, particularly those in rural or low-income urban areas.
- **NIH investment is minimal** (2 studies), suggesting a funding blind spot for independent community rehabilitation research.

---

### Enrollment Trends by Trial Phase

| Phase | Count | % of Total | Key Insight |
|---|---|---|---|
| **NA** (no phase / not specified) | 298 | 70.1% | Most studies lack clear phase designation — unclear rigor |
| **Unknown** | 106 | 24.9% | Sponsorship ambiguity prevents phase classification |
| **PHASE3** | 5 | 1.2% | Very late-stage efficacy trials — critical for evidence-based practice |
| **PHASE1** | 3 | 0.7% | Minimal early-stage safety testing of new devices |
| **PHASE2** | 5 | 1.2% | Limited dose/response/device optimization studies |
| **PHASE4** | 5 | 1.2% | Post-market surveillance almost absent |
| **EARLY_PHASE1** | 4 | 0.9% | Pioneering device research is rare |

#### 🔴 Underservice Gaps by Phase
- **Only 10 studies (2.4%) are early-phase (Phase 1/2/Early Phase 1)** — the pipeline for novel prosthetic technologies is extremely thin.
- **Phase 3 efficacy trials are nearly nonexistent (5 studies, 1.2%)**, meaning there is very little high-quality evidence to support prosthetic prescription and rehabilitation protocols.
- **298 studies (70.1%) have no phase at all**, suggesting poor trial design transparency and making it difficult for patients and clinicians to identify rigorous studies.
- **Phase 4 post-market studies are almost absent**, meaning long-term device safety and performance data is rarely collected after market release.

---

### Key Disparities Summary

| Underserved Group | Evidence of Gap |
|---|---|
| **Non-veteran civilians** | 9.6% FED-sponsored studies overwhelmingly VA-focused |
| **Rural prosthetic users** | Industry trials (13.6%) tend to concentrate in urban academic centers |
| **Children / pediatric** | Very few studies specify pediatric inclusion; most target ADULT/OLDER_ADULT |
| **Low-income / uninsured** | No sponsor category specifically addresses access equity |
| **Older adults with comorbidities** | Exclusion criteria in most trials filter out patients with vascular disease, ESRD, etc. |
| **Early-stage device innovation** | Only 0.7% Phase 1 — the innovation pipeline is starved |

## 3. Proposed Outreach Strategies

Based on the trial gaps and site accessibility analysis, we recommend the following strategies:

### Strategy 1: Bridge the Civilian-Veteran Gap
- **Partner with civilian rehab centers** (in addition to VA sites) to open non-VA-funded prosthetic trials.
- **Target NOWHERE-else**: Create a registry of civilians excluded from VA-only studies and connect them to industry-sponsored trials.
- **Outreach hub**: Use the Shirley Ryan AbilityLab as a recruitment and screening site for non-veteran prosthetic users.

### Strategy 2: Address the Early-Phase Pipeline Shortage
- **Advocate for Phase 1/2 funding** through NIH and device-company partnerships specifically targeting novel prosthetic technologies.
- **Host community info sessions** at the AbilityLab about ongoing trials, so prosthetic users are aware of early-stage opportunities.
- **Create a "Prosthetic Trial Navigator"** role to help users understand eligibility and enroll.

### Strategy 3: Improve Trial Design Inclusivity
- **Push for broader inclusion criteria**: Most trials exclude patients with vascular disease, ESRD, and cognitive conditions — populations that are disproportionately from underserved communities.
- **Require pediatric and older-adult representation** in trial protocols.
- **Phase designation transparency**: Insist that all published trials include clear phase labels so users can assess evidence quality.

### Strategy 4: Leverage the AbilityLab's Transit-Rich Location
- **Free/low-cost transit passes** for prosthetic users attending outreach sessions — address the parking fee barrier.
- **Monthly community drop-in hours** at the AbilityLab (using the wheelchair-accessible Panera or AMC venue nearby for overflow).
- **Tele-rehabilitation trial options**: For users who cannot travel, offer virtual screening and follow-up to reduce geographic barriers.

### Strategy 5: Fill the Pharmacy & Urgent Care Gap
- **On-site pharmacy partnership**: Work with a local pharmacy to provide prescription delivery or drop-off services at the AbilityLab.
- **Mobile clinic scheduling**: Bring urgent care services to the site during outreach events so prosthetic users don't miss trial appointments for basic medical needs.

### Strategy 6: Community Advisory Board
- **Establish a CAB of prosthetic users** from underrepresented groups (civilians, low-income, rural, pediatric, older adults) to guide trial design and outreach priorities.
- **Meet quarterly** at the AbilityLab or a nearby accessible venue (Panera is wheelchair accessible with Wi-Fi).
